## Full criticisms

### Opus 4.7 criticisms

- **[blocking] confound control: stimuli across conditions must be matched on dimensions not under test**
  - Evidence (simulated_walkthrough): *The human-framed condition ('Written by a staff journalist') is substantially shorter than the AI disclosure string, which introduces a confound the rehearsal cannot ignore: announcement duration differs across conditions by several seconds.*
  - Why: Dwell-onset latency, heading-jump counts following injection, and even think-aloud acknowledgment rates will all be inflated in the AI conditions purely because the participant is forced to wait through a longer announcement. A statistically significant Friedman result on any behavioral measure will be uninterpretable: the study cannot distinguish a semantic disclosure effect from an audio-interruption-length effect. The claimed contribution — that AI provenance disclosure shifts navigation and credibility — evaporates.
  - Required change: Either (a) length-match the disclosure strings to within 0.5 seconds of spoken duration (e.g., 'This article was AI-generated, human-edited' vs. 'This article was staff-written, human-edited'), verified with the actual target screen readers at participant-configured speech rates; or (b) add a fourth condition with a length-matched neutral announcement ('This article is approximately 800 words long') to isolate the interruption-length component; or (c) reframe the claim as 'disclosure presence vs. absence' and drop the human-framed condition entirely.

- **[blocking] statistical power adequate to the claim**
  - Evidence (prototype_spec): *Compare these measures across the three conditions (early-AI, late-AI, human) using a within-subject Friedman test across the 8 participants, with individual condition contrasts via Wilcoxon signed-rank.*
  - Why: With n=8 participants across three within-subject conditions, Friedman with post-hoc Wilcoxon has power to detect only very large effects (d > ~1.0). The one_sentence_claim asserts 'measurable' effects and that 'the effect depends on where the disclosure sits in the heading tree' — a three-way contrast requiring even more power than a two-way. A null result cannot distinguish 'no effect' from 'small-to-moderate effect the design cannot see,' yet the analysis plan presents these tests as if they can adjudicate the hypothesis. The qualitative signals cannot rescue the quantitative framing because the summed credibility score and derived latency measures are explicitly being tested for statistical significance.
  - Required change: Either (a) drop the inferential statistics entirely and reframe as a descriptive/qualitative evaluation with the quantitative measures reported as trial-level observations only; or (b) perform a sensitivity power analysis stating the minimum detectable effect size at n=8 and acknowledge in the contribution that only very large effects are addressable; or (c) increase n to a pre-specified target derived from a power analysis for the smallest effect of theoretical interest.

- **[major] demand characteristics control in within-subject designs**
  - Evidence (simulated_walkthrough): *by the third trial, a participant would reasonably have inferred that authorship framing is the experimental variable of interest, and their credibility ratings in trial 3 could plausibly reflect hypothesis-guessing rather than authentic judgment.*
  - Why: Within-subject exposure to three differently-framed disclosures on three otherwise-similar articles makes the manipulation transparent by trial 2 or 3. Latin-square counterbalancing addresses order but not hypothesis-guessing. The credibility ratings and section-selection choices in later trials will reflect what participants believe the researchers want to find, not authentic judgment. This directly undermines the credibility-rating dependent measure, which is one of the three central signals.
  - Required change: Move to a between-subjects design for the credibility rating (one condition per participant, requiring a larger n), OR add an explicit cover story and post-hoc suspicion probe before debrief to identify and exclude participants who inferred the manipulation, OR restrict the credibility-rating analysis to trial 1 only and acknowledge the remaining trials as exploratory qualitative data.

- **[major] qualitative analysis rigor: coder blinding and codebook development**
  - Evidence (prototype_spec): *Two coders independently apply a 4-category scheme to each clip... Compute inter-rater agreement (Cohen's kappa) on the 24 clips (8 participants × 3 trials).*
  - Why: The analysis plan names Cohen's kappa but does not specify: (a) whether coders are blinded to condition assignment when coding the 20-second clips — if the clip begins with the banner audio, the coder will hear which condition it is and blinding is impossible without audio editing; (b) how disagreements are resolved; (c) whether the codebook was developed on pilot data separate from the 24 analysis clips or will be refined iteratively on the same data (which inflates kappa). Without these specifications the qualitative inter-rater statistic is not interpretable.
  - Required change: Specify: (i) the banner audio will be excised from clips before coding to enable blinding, or state explicitly that blinding is impossible and justify; (ii) the disagreement-resolution protocol (e.g., third-coder adjudication); (iii) that the codebook will be finalized on pilot/warm-up data before the 24 analysis clips are coded, and report kappa on the locked codebook only.

- **[major] manipulation fidelity: the experimental condition must be delivered as specified**
  - Evidence (prototype_spec): *Determine from the screen-share and NVDA speech viewer overlay when the participant's virtual cursor has passed the second H2 heading, as the late-banner trigger timing depends on this cursor position rather than elapsed time.*
  - Why: The walkthrough notes that a fast navigator can traverse all three H2s in under 4 seconds, giving the wizard essentially no window to fire the late-banner between H2 #2 and H2 #3. When the wizard misses the window, the 'late' condition collapses into a variable-timing condition — sometimes after H2 #2, sometimes after H2 #3, sometimes during body reading. The branch's core claim that 'the effect depends on where the disclosure sits in the heading tree' is precisely a claim about timing, and timing is exactly what the wizard cannot reliably deliver. Manipulation fidelity is unverified.
  - Required change: Either (a) automate the late-banner trigger using a DOM-based heading-focus event listener (JavaScript focus/visibility tracking on the H2 element) rather than wizard-eyeballing the screen-share, with the wizard retaining only a veto override; or (b) log a per-trial manipulation-fidelity measure (seconds from second-H2-pass to banner-fire) and pre-register an exclusion threshold; or (c) drop the early/late distinction and test disclosure-presence vs. absence only.

- **[major] sampling adequacy to the population the claim addresses**
  - Evidence (branch_card): *does disclosing AI authorship via an ARIA-live provenance banner shift BLV screen-reader users' navigation strategies and credibility judgments*
  - Why: The claim is about 'BLV screen-reader users' as a population, but n=8 recruited from a single disability-led community organization cannot support a population-level claim. BLV screen-reader users vary enormously in assistive-tech configuration (NVDA vs. VoiceOver vs. JAWS), onset (congenital vs. acquired blindness), age, and browsing expertise. The sample is a convenience sample whose configurations are deliberately heterogeneous (each participant uses their own setup), which introduces variance that the n=8 cannot absorb. No stopping rule, saturation criterion, or sampling framework is stated.
  - Required change: Reframe the contribution explicitly as an exploratory case series (n=8 expert users from one community organization), drop population-level language, and state a qualitative saturation criterion (e.g., thematic saturation across the debrief corpus) rather than implying the findings generalize to BLV screen-reader users broadly. Alternatively, expand recruitment across multiple organizations and screen-reader platforms with a pre-specified stratification.

### Sonnet 4.6 criticisms

- **[blocking] confound architecture — a variable that explains the results equally well is not controlled**
  - Evidence (simulated_walkthrough): *The human-framed condition ('Written by a staff journalist') is substantially shorter than the AI disclosure string, which introduces a confound the rehearsal cannot ignore: announcement duration differs across conditions by several seconds. A participant in the AI condition would plausibly experience a longer interruption before resuming heading navigation, and any condition difference in dwell-onset latency could partly reflect announcement length rather than content.*
  - Why: The primary quantitative behavioral measure is 'time from page-load completion to first body-text dwell,' compared across conditions via Friedman test. Because the AI disclosure string is approximately three times longer than the human-framed string, any observed latency difference is causally ambiguous: it could reflect semantic processing of AI authorship, or simply the mechanical fact that a longer audio stream delays the moment the participant can resume heading navigation. No post-hoc analysis can disentangle these after data collection because announcement duration and semantic content are perfectly collinear within the design as written.
  - Required change: Equalize announcement duration across all three conditions by padding the human-framed string to the same word count (e.g., 'Written by a staff journalist who researched and fact-checked this article') or by measuring and covarying actual announcement duration in seconds as logged from the Zoom audio, with a supplementary analysis excluding trials where duration differed by more than one second from the condition mean.

- **[blocking] manipulation integrity — the independent variable cannot be delivered reliably across participants**
  - Evidence (simulated_walkthrough): *For the late-banner condition specifically, the wizard must detect in real time that the participant's cursor has passed the second H2, and then fire the injection — but a participant who jumps rapidly from H2 to H2 using the H key could plausibly traverse all three headings in under 4 seconds, giving the wizard almost no window to fire between the second and third heading. The late-banner timing precision would reasonably degrade under fast navigators, potentially collapsing the early/late distinction for a subset of trials.*
  - Why: The research question explicitly turns on whether banner placement relative to the heading tree ('where the disclosure sits in the heading tree') modulates navigation strategy. If the late-banner condition collapses into a randomly-timed or post-navigation injection for fast-navigating participants, the early-versus-late contrast — which is the mechanistic claim of the study — cannot be evaluated. A within-subject Friedman test on three conditions where one condition is operationally unstable will produce uninterpretable pairwise contrasts between early-AI and late-AI, which is the comparison that distinguishes this study from prior disclosure-timing literature.
  - Required change: Replace human-judgment trigger timing for the late condition with an automated cursor-position event: instrument the prototype HTML to emit a server-sent event when the screen reader's focus element matches the second H2's ID, and have the server auto-inject the late banner on that event. This removes wizard reaction-time variance entirely. Alternatively, define 'late' as a fixed elapsed-time delay (e.g., 15 seconds post-load) and pre-specify that trials where the participant reached the third H2 before the injection are excluded from the late-condition analysis with this exclusion criterion registered before data collection.

- **[major] demand characteristics — within-subject design with semantically transparent conditions produces hypothesis-confirming response bias**
  - Evidence (simulated_walkthrough): *by the third trial, a participant would reasonably have inferred that authorship framing is the experimental variable of interest, and their credibility ratings in trial 3 could plausibly reflect hypothesis-guessing rather than authentic judgment. The Latin-square counterbalancing addresses order effects at the group level but does not neutralize this within-participant awareness drift.*
  - Why: Each participant receives all three conditions, and the conditions differ only in announced authorship. By trial 2 or 3, a participant has already heard one or two disclosure variants and will reasonably infer that authorship is the variable being tested. The 7-item credibility scale then measures a mix of authentic credibility judgment and strategic responding. The debrief question 'whether the participant noticed any difference between articles' will retrospectively reveal how many participants guessed the manipulation — but by then the credibility data are already collected and cannot be corrected. With n=8 and all participants contributing trial-3 data, demand-characteristic contamination in the final condition of each participant's sequence is not a minor noise source; it is structurally guaranteed for at least the subset of participants who notice the pattern.
  - Required change: Add a post-experiment suspicion probe administered before the debrief explanation ('Did you form any impression of what the study was examining?') and pre-register an exclusion criterion: participants who correctly name authorship disclosure as the experimental variable before debriefing are excluded from credibility rating analysis. If anticipated exclusions would reduce analyzable n below 6, switch to a between-subjects design for the credibility measure while retaining within-subject counterbalancing only for the behavioral navigation measures.

- **[major] power analysis absent where effect claims are quantitative**
  - Evidence (prototype_spec): *Compare these measures across the three conditions (early-AI, late-AI, human) using a within-subject Friedman test across the 8 participants, with individual condition contrasts via Wilcoxon signed-rank.*
  - Why: The analysis plan specifies inferential tests (Friedman, Wilcoxon signed-rank) applied to n=8 with three conditions, producing 8 trial-triplets. No power analysis or effect-size justification appears anywhere in the prototype spec, branch card, or audit. With n=8, the Friedman test has very low power to detect anything below a large effect (f > 0.4 by conventional benchmarks). The one-sentence claim asserts 'measurable ways' of shifting navigation strategies and trust ratings — a quantitative claim — but the design as specified cannot reliably detect small or medium effects. A null result will be uninterpretable: it cannot distinguish 'disclosure has no effect' from 'this design was underpowered to detect a plausible effect.'
  - Required change: Either (a) conduct an a priori power analysis citing an effect-size estimate from the closest available prior work on disclosure and credibility judgment (e.g., Liang et al. 2024 cited in the branch card), determine the required n, and recruit accordingly, or (b) reframe the contribution explicitly as a qualitative feasibility study with no inferential claims, remove the Friedman and Wilcoxon tests from the analysis plan, and replace them with descriptive tabulation plus qualitative thematic analysis of think-aloud data. The current hybrid — small n with inferential statistics — satisfies neither standard.

- **[major] analysis plan placeholder — codebook reliability plan is post-hoc and thresholds for acceptable agreement are unspecified**
  - Evidence (prototype_spec): *Two coders independently apply a 4-category scheme to each clip: (A) no verbal acknowledgment of disclosure, (B) verbal acknowledgment without evaluative language (e.g., 'oh, it said something'), (C) verbal acknowledgment with positive or neutral evaluation ('okay, AI-assisted, fine'), (D) verbal acknowledgment with skeptical or adversarial language ('AI? Let me check the sources'). Compute inter-rater agreement (Cohen's kappa) on the 24 clips.*
  - Why: The 4-category scheme distinguishes B from C on the basis of 'evaluative language' and C from D on the basis of 'skeptical or adversarial language,' but no codebook defining these category boundaries is described in the materials list. The two coders will begin coding without calibration examples or anchor utterances, and Cohen's kappa is computed after coding is complete with no pre-specified minimum acceptable kappa and no protocol for adjudicating disagreements. If kappa falls below 0.60, the think-aloud signal — which is the primary evidence for the adversarial-check relational mode predicted by the branch hypothesis — is unreliable, but the proposal contains no stopping rule or fallback for this scenario.
  - Required change: Before data collection, produce a written codebook with at least three anchor examples per category drawn from pilot sessions or analogous transcripts, conduct a calibration pass where both coders apply the scheme to 5 practice clips and discuss disagreements until reaching consensus, and pre-register a minimum acceptable kappa of 0.70 with a specified adjudication procedure (e.g., third coder as tiebreaker) for clips that remain in disagreement after one reconciliation round.
