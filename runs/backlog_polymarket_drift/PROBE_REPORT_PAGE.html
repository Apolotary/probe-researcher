Premise

The researcher's original question asked how semi-automated trading agents should surface execution drift to users monitoring multiple markets simultaneously. [RESEARCHER_INPUT]

That question was sharpened during Probe to focus on a specific decision point: when a semi-automated agent exceeds its declared slippage tolerance on one of several concurrent prediction markets, does requiring the agent to present a structured counter-argument (reasons the user should NOT override) change whether override decisions are better calibrated than presenting drift evidence alone? [AGENT_INFERENCE]

This guidebook plans a formative evaluative study of a wizard-of-oz (WoZ) prototype that simulates the counter-argument mechanism. The study is designed to characterize override-decision behavior under a single, bounded manipulation; it is not designed to evaluate a deployable product nor to produce performance benchmarks. [AGENT_INFERENCE]

This study can support claims about how override decisions are made in a single laboratory session under the specific scripted conditions tested. [DO_NOT_CLAIM]

This study cannot support claims about long-term trader behavior, real-money decision-making, general trust in trading agents, or the effectiveness of counter-argument framing outside the tested drift magnitudes and market types. [DO_NOT_CLAIM]

This study cannot, in its current two-cell form, support causal claims about adversarial argumentation as the active ingredient, because the adversarial condition bundles modal interruption, argument structure, and adversarial framing into a single manipulation. This is a known unresolved design issue flagged in meta-review. [DO_NOT_CLAIM]

Background

Buijsman, Carter, and Bermúdez argue that AI decision-support can erode domain-specific autonomy through absent failure indicators and unconscious value shifts, and that design patterns such as defeater mechanisms are one way to preserve user agency at the point of action. [SOURCE_CARD:buijsman_carter_bermudez_2025]

Sundar's MAIN model describes how agency cues in a digital interface can invoke an authority heuristic or a "machine heuristic" that changes perceived trustworthiness independently of actual source quality, biasing credibility judgments in ways users do not notice. This framing motivates the study's concern that a structured counter-argument may produce deference rather than deliberation. [SOURCE_CARD:sundar_2008_main_model]

Longoni and colleagues found that resistance to AI recommendations is attenuated when the AI serves a supporting role to a human decision-maker rather than replacing one, suggesting that framing agent output as advisory rather than authoritative affects how users engage with it. The counter-argument framing tested here sits within this advisory space. [SOURCE_CARD:longoni_2019_medical_ai_resistance]

Jakesch and colleagues showed that built-in opinions of AI language technologies can shift users' views at scale even when users are unaware they are being influenced, underscoring the need to engineer the directional framing of AI-generated arguments carefully. The adversarial-framing condition in this study is precisely such a directional framing, and its effects should be interpreted against this capture-risk background. [SOURCE_CARD:jakesch_2023_cowriting]

The following outside-corpus literature was flagged during review as potentially adjacent but is NOT present in the Probe corpus. The researcher must verify each of these independently before grounding any design choice on them: [AGENT_INFERENCE]

  • Parasuraman's work on automation trust and supervisory control, cited in the premise-stage analysis as the nearest template for notification-design studies in multi-stream monitoring. [UNCITED_ADJACENT]
  • Jian, Bisantz, and Drury (2000) Trust in Automation scale, referenced in the prototype's questionnaire materials but not present as a source card. [UNCITED_ADJACENT]
  • NASA-TLX cognitive load subscale, referenced in the questionnaire but not present as a source card. [UNCITED_ADJACENT]

Method

Probe does not run a study. It writes one, rehearses it, and audits the rehearsal. This section restates the prototype specification — the artifact a different researcher could build from without asking clarifying questions — and the study protocol.

The prototype is a wizard-of-oz (WoZ) simulation of a semi-automated trading assistant monitoring six concurrent prediction markets. A single participant sits at a workstation and watches a 2x3 grid of market panels, each showing position size, entry price, current price, and a slippage indicator. A researcher in an adjacent room (the wizard) triggers scripted drift events at pre-set times. [AGENT_INFERENCE]

In the adversarial condition, when a drift alert fires, a modal dialogue overlays the affected market panel within 4 seconds. The modal header reads "Agent argues against override" and contains three structured components: a base rate reminder ("in X% of historical cases, drift at this magnitude reverted within Y hours"), a risk asymmetry note (what an unnecessary exit costs), and a confidence qualifier (how many comparable events support the estimate). Two buttons are available: "Override anyway" and "Accept agent recommendation." [AGENT_INFERENCE]

In the neutral condition, the same drift alert fires, but no modal appears. The alert banner shows the raw drift percentage, the threshold, and the same back-test base rate number with no framing or argument structure. [AGENT_INFERENCE]

Each session runs for 75 minutes: a 10-minute briefing and consent block, a 10-minute practice block on a stable 4-market dashboard, a 40-minute main task block with 8 scripted drift events (4 genuine drift, 4 false-alarm), a 10-minute post-task questionnaire, and a 5-minute debrief including the WoZ reveal. [AGENT_INFERENCE]

The wizard has five controls: InjectDriftAlert, InjectCounterArgumentTemplate (selecting from a 12-template library indexed by market type × drift magnitude), PauseCountdownTimer, ResumeCountdownTimer, and ForceResetMarketPanel. The counter-argument templates are pre-authored and selected in real time rather than generated. [AGENT_INFERENCE]

Observable signals include: override decision accuracy per trial (against scripted ground truth), decision latency from alert to click, think-aloud references to argument components, post-task Trust-in-Automation Likert ratings, post-session semi-structured interview responses, and wizard/observer logs of rapid-dismiss events and technical errors. [AGENT_INFERENCE]

Capture-risk audit

Probe fires sixteen named patterns across four axes — Capacity, Agency, Exit, Legibility — against every branch's prototype spec and simulated walkthrough. A pattern is only reported when the auditor can quote an evidence span from the rehearsed artifact; patterns that cannot be grounded in a specific passage are not reported. A -2 score blocks the branch.

legibility.no_failure_signal legibility score -2 branch B
source: simulated_walkthrough
A participant whose calibration is marginal but accepted would plausibly experience dwell-triggered promotions that feel mistimed, and would have no way to attribute this to calibration rather than to the rule logic.

When the gaze-tracking or wizard judgment is wrong (missed dwell, mistimed promotion, wizard-serialization lag under high load, calibration drift), there is no in-flow signal to the participant that anything has failed. The walkthrough identifies this explicitly as contamination of the Likert trust item. No observable_signal or wizard_control captures participant detection of escalation errors; errors are silently absorbed into the rule-endorsement signal the study is trying to measure.

capacity.no_fade_mechanism capacity score -1 branch A
source: prototype_spec
In the ADVERSARIAL CONDITION: within 4 seconds of the alert appearing, a modal dialogue box overlays the market panel... Two buttons remain available: 'Override anyway' and 'Accept agent recommendation'.

The counter-argument modal fires at a fixed intensity across all 4 adversarial trials with no mechanism to reduce assistance as the participant demonstrates calibrated judgement. A participant who makes two correct decisions in a row still receives the full three-part structured argument on trial three. This is acceptable within a 75-minute evaluative session but would be a capture risk at scale.

agency.hides_options_before_review agency score -1 branch A
source: prototype_spec
Two buttons remain available: 'Override anyway' and 'Accept agent recommendation'.

The modal presents a single framed recommendation ('Agent argues against override') with no exposure of the decision space the agent itself considered — the space of alternative market states, the templates not selected, or the evidence weighted against the recommendation. The participant sees the agent's conclusion and its rhetoric but not the alternatives the agent ranked below its recommendation.

legibility.opaque_ranking legibility score -1 branch A
source: prototype_spec
Wizard selects a template from a dropdown in the wizard control panel after clicking 'Fire' on InjectDriftAlert but before the modal renders... the auto-selection algorithm matches event market type and drift band to the closest template.

The counter-argument template shown to the participant is selected from a 12-template library by an auto-selection algorithm (with wizard override), but the participant is shown only the chosen template with no indication that selection occurred or on what basis. The argument is presented as if it were the agent's unique response to this specific event, when in fact it is a match from a small library, and this selection logic is invisible at the point of decision.

legibility.no_failure_signal legibility score -1 branch A
source: prototype_spec
The wizard selects the wrong counter-argument template for the current event's market type or drift band... causing the argument content shown to the participant to be factually inconsistent with the scenario's back-test statistics displayed elsewhere on the dashboard.

The failure_case anticipates template-mismatch scenarios where the agent's argument is factually wrong relative to the sparkline, but there is no in-dashboard signal to flag the agent as unreliable — detection depends on the participant cross-checking the sparkline. The simulated walkthrough explicitly flags that sparkline-checkers are a self-selected subset. Additionally, in the 4 false-alarm trials the agent's confidence framing gives no indication that this event-class has historically fewer than 20 comparable cases beyond the 'confidence qualifier' sentence, which is itself part of the manipulation.

legibility.unverifiable_summary legibility score -1 branch A
source: prototype_spec
In 73% of historical cases where slippage reached 7–8% on binary election markets, the price reverted within 2 hours without intervention

The base rate claim is presented as a summarised statistic without an inline mechanism to inspect the underlying 41-event corpus. The sparkline on the market panel offers partial verification but the walkthrough flags that cross-checking is optional, effortful, and unlikely within the 90-second window. The participant is expected to act on a synthesised number with no 'show me the 41 cases' affordance at the point of action.

wizard_controls: ESCALATE ... FORCE_FOVEAL ... triggers ... rulebook specifies trigger FORCE_FOVEAL only if severity has crossed 85.

Escalation assistance fires at fixed rule thresholds throughout the 45-minute main task with no mechanism to attenuate assistance as the participant demonstrates competence (e.g., if they are successfully scanning a market, the ESCALATE rule does not back off). legibility.no_failure_signal legibility score -1 branch C
source: simulated_walkthrough
The Suppress Alert (Hold) control depends on participants noticing an *absence*, which is a weaker signal than noticing a presence; the weekly reflection prompts are the main capture mechanism, and a participant who simply doesn't mention the non-alert produces ambiguous data

When the system (via wizard) fails to flag a market move the participant's contract should have caught — or conversely over-flags — there is no in-flow signal surfacing the mismatch. Participants are expected to detect absences of alerts through their own inspection, and the analysis acknowledges this absence-detection is weaker than presence-detection. Error detection is deferred to offline vignette tasks, leaving real-time failures silent.

capacity.no_rehearsal_space capacity score 1 branch C
source: prototype_spec
Week 0 onboarding session (90 min in-person): ... 30 min guided contract authoring for 2 assigned synthetic markets, 15 min think-aloud baseline drift-prediction task on 3 held-out episodes

The onboarding session explicitly includes a 30-minute guided authoring phase on assigned synthetic markets and a 15-minute baseline drift-prediction task on held-out episodes before the four-week live phase begins. This is a low-stakes rehearsal space where participants can develop the contract-authoring skill before any consequential monitoring begins, scaffolding rather than suppressing capacity.

exit.competence_decay_on_exit exit score 1 branch C
source: prototype_spec
15 min drift-prediction accuracy task on 8 held-out episodes (primary outcome measure) ... static printed vignettes ... participants are shown 5 held-out episode vignettes (brief text + probability chart excerpt, no agent status shown) and asked to predict, for each, whether their agent would have flagged it

The primary outcome measure is explicitly an unaided-performance task: participants predict drift classifications on static printed vignettes without access to the dashboard or agent status, isolating their own declarative understanding of their rules. The vignette and card-sort tasks are a direct measurement of what the participant retains when the tool is removed, actively scaffolding exit-capacity assessment.

agency.weak_override agency score 2 branch C
source: prototype_spec
Participant edits their drift contract for at least one market after observing one or more agent alerts over the preceding days. The participant navigates to the contract editor, reads their prior version in the history panel, and modifies one or more fields.

Override here is substantive, not ceremonial: the participant can rewrite the governing rules at any time through a prominent editor, with versioned history and diff-highlighting available for reference. The revision path is not buried behind warnings or advanced menus, and the system does not frame revision as deviant — it is the central expected behavior of the protocol, making this a cultivation of agency.

legibility.no_rationale_at_point_of_action legibility score 2 branch C
source: prototype_spec
If wizard triggers 'Drift Detected,' the badge turns amber, a timestamped alert appears in the notification drawer listing the specific rule from the contract that was triggered, and the chart highlights the triggering price window in yellow.

At the point of action — when a drift alert fires — the notification drawer inline cites the specific contract clause that was triggered and the chart highlights the price window that triggered it. The participant can see the rationale for the agent's behavior at the moment they are deciding whether to acknowledge, revise, or ignore, satisfying the requirement for rationale before commitment. This is an explicit cultivation of legibility.

Reviewer panel

Three Opus 4.7 agents review the surviving branch in sequence — a methodologist, an accessibility advocate, and (optionally) a novelty hawk. A meta-reviewer reads their outputs and classifies the disagreement without collapsing it to consensus.

Meta-reviewer verdict: human_judgment_required (legitimate_methodological_split)

Both reviewers converge on major_revision as a recommendation, but their decisive blockers target non-overlapping parts of the design. The methodologist's blockers (design confounding, power, ground-truth construction) go to whether the study can test its stated claim at all. The accessibility advocate's blocker (colour-only slippage indicator with no built alternative) goes to whether the measurement apparatus itself validly captures decisions for the full trader population. Resolving the methodologist's confound (adding arms, specifying power, deriving ground truth empirically) does not fix the indicator; rebuilding the indicator does not address the 2-cell confound. Neither reviewer refutes the other, and the fixes interact (e.g., adding arms multiplies the accessibility cost of not fixing the indicator first, and indicator-version may need to enter the power calculation as a covariate). The ordering and scope of these fixes is a design decision that should not be delegated.

ReviewerRecommendationDecisive weakness
methodologist major_revision The two-cell design confounds modal interruption, argument structure, and adversarial framing, so no outcome can be attributed to the counter-argument mechanism the claim names. Compounded by absent power analysis, researcher-stipulated calibration ground truth, and wizard intrusion on a key secondary DV.
accessibility_advocate major_revision The primary slippage indicator is encoded entirely in red/green, and the only mitigation for colour-blind participants is an unspecified 'flag for facilitator' workaround. The accessibility alternative is not built into the prototype, producing either silent exclusion or confounded decision data for ~8% of male traders.

What humans must decide

Every Probe guidebook ends with a [HUMAN_REQUIRED] block. This is not a closing pleasantry — it is the set of decisions the pipeline is not authorized to make on the researcher's behalf.

Before this study can be run, the researcher must resolve several decisions that Probe cannot resolve on behalf of the research program. Specifically: (1) decide whether to add a third (and fourth) experimental arm to isolate argument content from modal interruption, or instead to narrow the claim to "adversarial modal intervention" without a mechanistic attribution; (2) derive the 8 trial labels from a documented empirical back-test or reframe the DV away from calibration; (3) complete a power analysis tied to a pre-specified minimum effect of interest and adjust sample size or analysis unit accordingly; (4) build the shape-coded/pattern-coded slippage indicator as the default prototype affordance and pilot it with colour-blind traders before recruitment proceeds; (5) specify compensation, recruitment channel, and operational trading-frequency thresholds in writing; (6) author the interview codebook with exemplars and inter-rater reliability procedure; (7) pre-register the full analysis plan including trial-1 handling, rapid-dismiss post-hoc coding, and any covariates introduced by indicator-version piloting; (8) consult with an IRB or equivalent ethics body on the revised consent language, debrief distress protocol, and deception structure. The meta-review explicitly declined to resolve the ordering of these fixes because the methodology and accessibility blockers interact and the trade-offs are a research-program decision, not a Probe decision.
