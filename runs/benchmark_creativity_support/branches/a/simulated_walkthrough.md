## Session opening

A poet arriving for this 90-minute session would plausibly enter the lab room carrying expectations set by the recruitment materials — that they are trying a drafting tool, not that they are being evaluated. The first ten minutes of briefing and consent would likely be uneventful in themselves, but the warm-up poem task could become the moment where the WoZ illusion is first stress-tested; a participant who happens to write a warm-up line in an unusual register might expose the wizard's preparation latency earlier than anticipated. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The consent form's request for publication venues and rights to prior published text would reasonably prompt some participants to pause and read carefully, since poets often have complicated feelings about how their prior work is used. This would plausibly lengthen the 0–10 min window for a non-trivial fraction of sessions, which could compress the warm-up and increase the risk of the participant entering Poem A without a settled understanding of the interface. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A facilitator introducing the condition order at 10–15 min would likely need to narrate the tool in generic terms ("a drafting assistant that suggests line endings") to avoid tipping off the corpus-personalization mechanism. Under this framing, a participant might reasonably form a mental model closer to an autocomplete tool, which could shape how they interpret suggestions they encounter in the first condition more than researchers might assume. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Task flow walkthrough

### Step 1 — Reading the prompt and typing the first line

A poet encountering Prompt A ("an object you no longer own") would plausibly spend anywhere from thirty seconds to several minutes in silent reading before touching the keyboard, and the wizard's rule of waiting for "a partial first line" before enabling the suggestion button would likely hold during this interval — but only if the participant types linearly. A poet who types a phrase, deletes it, types another, and paces that way for two minutes would create ambiguity for the wizard about when exactly to arm the control. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

Friction at this step would most plausibly concentrate in the wizard's mental workload rather than the participant's experience. The wizard is simultaneously loading the correct candidate bank, reading the participant's emerging line via VNC, and deciding when the button is ready — all before the participant has any reason to click it. A wizard who has not yet finished loading the bank when the participant clicks early would be forced into `extend_latency` on the very first request, which is the fidelity event most likely to seed suspicion because it arrives before the participant has established a baseline for how fast the tool "usually" responds. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 2 — Requesting candidates

Reaching step 2, a participant who has just finished a line would likely click the button expecting something like a machine response; the 1.5–2.5 second latency would plausibly feel natural for a poet familiar with GPT-style tools but slightly slow for a poet familiar with local autocomplete. This asymmetry is worth rehearsing because it means the WoZ illusion is probably more robust for participants whose prior tool exposure skews toward cloud-based assistants. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's decision task at this step is the highest-stakes cognitive load in the entire protocol. For the corpus-divergence condition, the wizard must — in roughly 1.5 seconds — read the semantic content of the just-typed line via VNC, map it to a semantic domain cluster among the 20+ pre-built clusters, and pick 5 candidates meeting both the low-frequency and phonological-distinctness criteria. A rehearsal of this step strongly suggests that the 1.5-second window would be insufficient for a substantial share of requests, meaning `extend_latency` would plausibly fire more often than the protocol's 20%-exclusion threshold anticipates. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant might reasonably expect, after two or three requests, that the response time is roughly constant. If the wizard's latency extensions are not randomized across both "need extension" and "don't need extension" cases, the extended-latency events could form a pattern a sharp participant detects — e.g., longer delays happening specifically on their more unusual lines, which is precisely when a real model would likely respond faster (having more common words available) or slower (having fewer training examples), neither matching the wizard's actual dependency. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 3 — Accept or reject

At the moment of reviewing 5 chips, a poet would plausibly experience this as a moment of micro-decision that is much denser than the interface design suggests. With no score, rank, or provenance shown, the chips carry only their text, which means the participant's decision leans heavily on aesthetic reaction within a few seconds. This pressure could produce friction for poets who habitually deliberate over single words at length — the compact chip UI could feel like it is nudging them toward speed rather than reflection. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A reject event carries real interpretive ambiguity that the protocol does not fully resolve. A dismissal could plausibly mean "none of these fit," "I had already decided my ending before I clicked," "I don't like suggestions in principle and clicked out of curiosity," or "the first chip caught my eye but I pulled back." The analysis plan treats accepts and rejects as categorical events, but the information needed to interpret rejects probably lives in the think-aloud audio rather than the event stream. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's side-task of annotating whether the accepted candidate was the highest-divergence or lowest-divergence of the 5 queued would plausibly be the wizard's most-skipped duty under load, because it arrives precisely when the wizard must also prepare for the next request. A rehearsal of this step suggests this annotation is likely to be incomplete for exactly the sessions where it matters most — high-request-rate sessions with sharp participants. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 4 — Continued drafting cycle

Across a 25-minute drafting period, a poet would likely settle into one of several distinguishable patterns: a "high-request" pattern where they ask for suggestions on most lines, a "sparse" pattern where they ask only when stuck, or an "early-abandonment" pattern where they try the feature once or twice and then draft freely. Only the high-request pattern generates enough data for the primary divergence measures to reach statistical power on a per-participant basis, which means the study's yield per session is probably highly bimodal. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The 90-second pause rule for facilitator intervention ("Take as long as you need; just keep drafting") would plausibly feel intrusive to poets whose drafting rhythm naturally includes long silences for line revision in their head. A participant might reasonably experience this prompt as evaluative pressure — as if they are being watched for productivity — which could shift their subsequent behavior toward more requests as a way of appearing engaged. This would contaminate the acceptance-rate measure in a direction the analysis plan does not correct for. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's "phonological entrainment" flag — where a participant appears to be echoing the phonology of a recently inserted candidate into a downstream line — asks the wizard to make a fairly sophisticated real-time judgment while also selecting fresh candidates for that very downstream line. It seems plausible that this flag would fire only when the wizard happens to be between requests, not when entrainment actually occurs, which would introduce a systematic bias toward detecting entrainment during low-activity stretches. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 5 — Finish, survey, and export

A participant clicking "Finish poem" or hearing the 25-minute signal would plausibly experience a small relief-and-deflation moment, and the transition to a paper Likert survey would change the cognitive mode abruptly. Self-report items administered seconds after drafting would likely capture something closer to an affective reaction to the just-completed poem than a stable judgment about stylistic distance — particularly the "This poem surprised me" item, where "surprise" is ambiguous between "the tool surprised me" and "my own writing surprised me." [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

Reaching the export step, the silent log-file check via VNC is the protocol's single point of highest technical fragility. Failure at this step would likely cascade, because the fallback bookmarklet depends on the participant not navigating away, but a participant who has just finished a poem might reasonably close the browser tab or scroll away out of habit before the facilitator retrieves the file. The compound-failure scenario the spec flags is therefore not an edge case — it is a plausibly recurrent outcome for a measurable share of sessions. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Friction hotspots

- The wizard's real-time cognitive load during corpus-divergence condition candidate selection is the highest-risk zone in the whole protocol; it is reasonable to expect `extend_latency` to fire far more often than anticipated, potentially on a majority of requests for prolific poets with narrow indexed corpora. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The chip display carries no provenance or ranking, so a participant's accept/reject decision compresses a great deal of tacit reasoning into a click that the event stream cannot decode on its own; the interpretive burden falls back onto think-aloud audio that many poets will plausibly not produce spontaneously. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The counterbalanced within-subjects design places both conditions in a single 90-minute session, which introduces fatigue and carryover — a poet who drafts with corpus-divergence candidates in Poem A is likely to carry some of that lexical territory into Poem B even under the thesaurus condition. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The facilitator's 90-second pause prompt risks converting natural reflective silence into perceived evaluation pressure, plausibly inflating suggestion-request rates in a way that contaminates between-condition acceptance comparisons. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The JSON export at Step 5 has multiple plausible failure modes (0-byte file, participant navigation away, bookmarklet timing), and the protocol's deepest fallback (manual sessionStorage recovery with the participant still seated) would itself plausibly break the WoZ illusion because it requires the wizard to enter the room and open developer tools. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Risks to interpretation

The divergence score computed from accepted candidates assumes the acceptance event cleanly reflects the participant's endorsement of that candidate, but a rehearsal suggests acceptances are likely to include a meaningful share of "tried it to see how it reads" events where the participant inserts a candidate, keeps drafting, and may or may not delete it later. If the log captures only the accept-insertion event and not subsequent deletions, the divergence measure would systematically overstate the effect because trial-insertions would be weighted equivalently to committed choices. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The think-aloud audio signal carries a well-known risk that the protocol's analysis plan treats more lightly than the literature would suggest: poets are a population who are often reflectively verbal about word choice, but they are also a population practiced at silent private deliberation. It is plausible that habitualness commentary is distributed very unevenly across participants — a few will narrate extensively, most will not — which would make the habitualness-to-pragmatic ratio a noisy between-subjects measure even with 20% inter-rater reliability checks. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The corpus index itself carries interpretive weight that the spec hedges but does not fully resolve. A poet's "indexed prior work" is bounded by whatever the poet provided and whatever was traceable; if a poet's most experimental work is unpublished or published in venues that were not indexed, the low-frequency candidates drawn for them could plausibly match their own unindexed experimental register closely, producing apparent "divergence from corpus" that is in fact convergence with their broader practice. This would systematically attenuate the measured effect in directions that look like null findings. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The WoZ illusion is probably not equally fragile across poet profiles. A poet who writes frequently about AI, computation, or language models would plausibly probe the system's responses more skeptically and could surface the latency-dependency pattern described above within the first few requests; a poet whose practice is oriented elsewhere would plausibly not. This means detected-deception events are likely non-random with respect to participant characteristics in ways that could correlate with the divergence measures themselves. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
