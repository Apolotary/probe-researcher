## Session opening

A participant arriving at the university writing center for this study would likely encounter the facilitator first, in a hallway or small reception area, before entering the drafting room itself. Given that the cover story frames the tool as "a language model fine-tuned on formal verse," the participant's mental model entering the session would plausibly already be oriented toward evaluating a machine system, which is the intended framing but which also means the participant brings whatever priors they hold about AI writing tools into the very first condition. A participant who has strong prior skepticism about AI-generated verse might reasonably begin the first co-writer condition in a more adversarial stance than a participant who is curious or neutral, and this pre-session disposition would not be captured by any of the current measurement instruments. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The 5-item prosody screening quiz sits in a delicate position: it is gating, but it is also a prime. A participant who completes the quiz would likely have metrical pattern-matching heightened in working memory going into the first drafting task, which could plausibly sharpen their scrutiny of candidate scansion during the co-writer condition in ways that would not occur in a naturalistic use context. This priming effect is probably unavoidable given the competence-screening purpose, but it is worth noting that the screening quiz and the dependent measures are not epistemically independent. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The warm-up phase places the facilitator in the room demonstrating the interface in whichever condition comes first per counterbalancing. A participant whose first condition is solo or rhyming-dictionary would never see the wizard-driven candidate panel during the warm-up, which means their first encounter with the co-writer panel would come cold, forty minutes or more into a session, and the absence of a practice round for the co-writer interface specifically could produce a first-line awkwardness that is not really about the tool but about unfamiliarity. This asymmetry across counterbalanced orders would plausibly introduce a condition-by-order interaction that the current analysis plan models via poem-order covariate but may not fully absorb. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Task flow walkthrough

### Step 1 — Prompt reading and line stem initiation

A participant reading the prompt card would likely spend between thirty seconds and two minutes simply absorbing the theme constraint "threshold," because the word is semantically broad and a formally trained poet would reasonably want to narrow it to a specific image before typing. This pre-typing cognitive work is invisible to the log events: the system only records from first keystroke onward, which means the "drafting duration" signal would systematically undercount the generative labor that happens before the fingers move. A participant who stares at the ceiling for ninety seconds and then writes a sonnet quickly would look, in the data, identical to a participant who types immediately but revises throughout. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The Tab-to-request-suggestions gesture is the hinge of the entire protocol, and it would plausibly feel unnatural to at least some participants. Most word processors treat Tab as indentation or focus movement, not as a semantic signal that one wants help. A participant might reasonably press Tab experimentally during the warm-up, then either internalize the convention or find it subtly alienating throughout the session. There is also the question of participants who habitually compose full lines before pausing: such a participant would have no natural "stem moment" at which to request suggestions, and would either have to artificially truncate their composition to invoke the tool or simply not use it at all, producing a near-total override condition that might misrepresent the tool's value. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The randomized 1–4 second spinner overlay is intended to mask wizard latency, but a working poet attentive to timing would plausibly notice that the delay is variable rather than constant, and variable delay is itself a cue that something adaptive is happening. A participant could reasonably infer that the system is "thinking harder" on some inputs, which is not what a fine-tuned language model would typically feel like at the latency scale participants experience from commercial tools. This is one of several places where the WoZ illusion is probably more fragile than the protocol assumes. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 2 — Accept, edit, or override cycle

At the moment three candidates appear, a participant would likely scan them in order and assess the plain/elevated/unexpected register spread before making a decision, but the interface does not label the registers to the participant — the register tags live only in the wizard's dispatch payload. A participant who happens to perceive and articulate the register dimension would be doing so via their own aesthetic sensibility, while a participant who does not perceive it would be treating the three candidates as an unstructured menu. This means the "unexpected" register candidate, which carries the most generative weight in the study's theoretical framing, could plausibly be systematically underused simply because participants do not have a cognitive handle for why it is there. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The edit pathway — accept then modify — is probably the richest behavioral signal in the study, because it captures a participant treating a candidate as scaffolding rather than as a finished product. Reaching this pathway, a participant would likely experience a small decision cost: the yellow highlight and editable state suggest the system is inviting modification, but a participant under time pressure or in flow might reasonably skip the edit and accept verbatim even when they have reservations. The edit/accept distinction would therefore plausibly compress under cognitive load, which means the edit rate might systematically underestimate participants' actual dissatisfaction with candidates as drafted. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The override pathway reveals an interface asymmetry worth flagging. When a participant overrides, the panel stays visible for five seconds with grayed-out candidates before collapsing. A participant might reasonably experience this as mild judgmental presence — the rejected candidates lingering in peripheral vision while they type their own ending — and this lingering could plausibly suppress override behavior among participants who are conflict-averse or who read the grayed panel as passive pressure. A cleaner design might collapse the panel immediately on override; the five-second lingering is a design choice whose behavioral consequences are themselves a testable question. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 3 — Submission and thirty-second read

The enforced thirty-second silent read before the questionnaire button activates is a thoughtful design choice, but it creates a specific psychological moment: the participant is confronted with the whole poem, decontextualized from the line-by-line drafting experience, immediately before being asked to rate their satisfaction. A participant encountering a poem that looks better as a whole than it felt to draft, or worse as a whole than it felt line-by-line, would plausibly have their Likert responses shifted by this final-view impression more than by the cumulative process. This is not a flaw — it is arguably closer to how poets actually evaluate their work — but it does mean the satisfaction rating is probably more about the artifact than about the experience. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

For the wizard's decision about whether to mark the session for prosody audit, the threshold of "3 or more uncorrected metrical breaks" relies on the wizard's real-time scansion being accurate across a two-hour attention span. A wizard operating in hour two, across a rare-rhyme passage, would plausibly have degraded scansion accuracy compared to hour one, which means the audit-flag decision carries embedded fatigue drift that could interact with condition order in ways the counterbalancing does not address. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 4 — Questionnaire and break

The 7-point Likert items on agency, fluency, satisfaction, perceived quality, and tool intrusiveness are each defensible individually, but administered immediately after a silent read of the completed poem, they would likely all correlate strongly with perceived-quality-of-the-artifact rather than capturing distinct constructs. A participant who liked their sonnet would plausibly rate everything positively and a participant who disliked it would rate everything negatively, collapsing the five-item structure toward a single general-evaluation factor. This is a common Likert problem but worth anticipating: the agency/intrusiveness distinction in particular, which the composite score leans on, could easily fail to emerge as separable. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The five-minute eyes-closed break between conditions would plausibly not reset cognitive state as cleanly as the protocol assumes. A formal-verse poet who has just spent forty minutes scanning iambic pentameter would probably have metrical pattern-matching active for well beyond five minutes; the second condition's baseline mental state is therefore not independent of the first condition, and the random effect for participant will absorb some but not all of this carryover. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 5 — Interview and debrief

The interview's fifth question — "Would you use a tool like this in your own practice?" — is asked before the WoZ debrief, which means participants are answering about the tool they believe they used, which is a fine-tuned language model. After the debrief, their answer would plausibly change, sometimes substantially, because "would I use a prosody-trained AI" and "would I use a human prosody collaborator on demand" are meaningfully different questions. The protocol captures the pre-debrief answer but not a post-debrief re-elicitation, which leaves the adoption-intent signal tethered to a false mental model. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The debrief itself is likely to produce strong affective reactions in at least some participants — surprise, a sense of having been deceived, occasionally a retrospective re-evaluation of their own behavior during the session. A participant who feels embarrassed about having anthropomorphized the "tool" in their post-condition open-ended responses might plausibly be reluctant to let those responses stand, even though withdrawal is offered. Planning for this affective arc, and documenting how participants respond, is probably itself a useful side-signal for future iterations. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Friction hotspots

- The Tab convention for invoking suggestions would likely produce early-session confusion in participants who do not naturally pause at line stems; the warm-up may not be sufficient to fully naturalize this gesture, especially in the counterbalanced orders where the co-writer condition comes second or third. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The absence of register labels on the three candidates in the participant-facing interface means the intended plain/elevated/unexpected design structure is probably invisible to participants, which could under-utilize the "unexpected" candidate that carries the most theoretical weight in the study. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The 1–4 second variable spinner overlay, intended to mask wizard latency, could plausibly itself become a suspicion cue because commercial language models typically produce near-constant latency at similar payload sizes; participants who have used such tools might reasonably notice the variance. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The grayed-out candidate panel lingering for five seconds after override could suppress override behavior through mild social-presence pressure, which would inflate accept rates artificially and distort the accept/edit/override signal. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The thirty-second enforced silent read before the questionnaire would plausibly compress the five Likert items toward a single artifact-evaluation factor, undermining the agency/intrusiveness composite. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The interview's adoption-intent question asked before debrief captures attitudes toward an imagined AI tool, not toward the actual (human-expert-on-demand) system the participant interacted with, which limits what the adoption signal can be taken to mean. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Wizard fatigue across a two-hour session, compounded across multiple participants per week, would plausibly degrade scansion accuracy and candidate-register discrimination in ways that interact with condition order and with the rare-rhyme failure case. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Risks to interpretation

The study compares three conditions — co-writer, solo, and rhyming-dictionary — but the rhyming-dictionary condition uses an embedded iframe of RhymeZone, which is a fundamentally different interaction mode (search-and-browse) than the push-based candidate delivery of the co-writer. A participant's behavior in the rhyming-dictionary condition would plausibly be shaped as much by the interface modality (pull vs. push) as by the informational content, which means a difference in craft scores between co-writer and rhyming-dictionary conditions would be ambiguous between "the tool helped because of what it offered" and "the tool helped because the participant did not have to interrupt composition to go fetch it." [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The nine-participant sample size with three within-subjects conditions yields twenty-seven poems, and the mixed-effects analysis with participant and rater crossed random effects is the right analytic choice for this design, but the statistical power to detect anything but large effects is limited. A plausible reading of null or ambiguous results would be that the study was simply too small, and this should be anticipated and framed in the analytic pre-registration as a descriptive-plus-effect-size report rather than a hypothesis test. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The WoZ illusion is load-bearing for the interpretation of the co-writer condition: if participants suspect the system is human-operated, they plausibly engage with it as a collaborator rather than as a tool, and the entire construct of "co-writing with AI" shifts under them. The protocol's suspicion-flagging is careful, but suspicion is often not binary or discretely timestamped — a participant may drift into half-belief without noticing, and self-report on the debrief form would plausibly be unreliable about when exactly the shift occurred. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The blind rater scheme assumes that condition-linked stylistic signatures do not leak into the poems themselves, but a co-writer condition poem that uses three wizard-supplied line endings with a consistent elevated register might plausibly read differently enough from a solo poem that an attentive rater could guess the condition above chance, even without labels. If condition-guessability by raters is non-trivial, the "blind" rating is not fully blind and the ICC-based reliability metric does not detect this. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

- Is the "unexpected" register candidate selected at rates meaningfully different from the plain and elevated candidates, and does its selection correlate with higher blind craft ratings on the imagistic specificity dimension specifically? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

- When participants edit an accepted candidate, is the edit distance systematically larger in the final quatrain and couplet (where formalist pressure is highest) than in the opening quatrain, which would suggest candidate utility varies with poem position? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
