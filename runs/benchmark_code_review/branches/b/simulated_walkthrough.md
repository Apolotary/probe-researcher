## Session opening

A participant arriving at this 90-minute session would plausibly enter with a mental model shaped by their existing team's code review norms, which may or may not align with the framing the facilitator introduces. The cover story — that they are evaluating "an experimental AI-assisted code review tool" — would likely activate expectations drawn from tools like GitHub Copilot or CodeRabbit, which lean toward suggestion-style assistance; the adversarial challenger condition would therefore likely feel genre-breaking in a way that could produce either productive surprise or defensive withdrawal, and the direction is not predictable in advance. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

During the 10-minute consent and baseline phase, a participant would plausibly fill out the 10-item Reviewer Self-Efficacy Scale quickly, and a risk worth naming is that self-efficacy ratings given before any review activity could be anchored on abstract self-image rather than on the specific legacy-code task at hand, which may reduce their value as a baseline covariate. The facilitator's instruction to think aloud could reasonably feel unnatural to engineers whose normal review practice is silent annotation, and the 30-second silence trigger for think-aloud prompts might feel intrusive to participants who read code in longer silent passes. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The 5-minute warm-up on a trivial 20-line bug fix would likely familiarize the participant with the confidence slider and comment box mechanics, but it would plausibly not prepare them for the experience of the mandatory response gate, because the warm-up is deliberately in no-tool mode. A participant who first encounters the gated adversarial condition could reasonably interpret the locked Approve button as a bug rather than a feature for the first several seconds, which is a concrete moment worth observing. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Task flow walkthrough

### Step 1 — Reading the diff and rating confidence

A participant encountering a 150–250 line legacy Python or Java diff cold would likely spend the first 2–4 minutes orienting to the file structure and the purpose of the change before producing a first comment, which compresses the effective review window per PR to roughly 16 minutes; under this constraint, pacing pressure could plausibly push participants toward higher-confidence snap judgments than they would make in their own work environment. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The confidence slider placed directly below each comment box would likely be engaged inconsistently across participants: some would reasonably treat it as a required form field and rate every comment deliberately, while others might default to a middle value (3) to avoid commitment, which would create a ceiling effect that dampens the very signal the Brier score analysis depends on. A rehearsal concern is that the 1–5 scale may be too coarse to distinguish "calibrated confidence" from "default middle setting," and a 7-point scale with anchored labels might produce more analytic resolution. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The 2–4 second "Analyzing..." spinner would likely read as plausible AI processing time to participants accustomed to LLM latency, but under repeated exposure across 15+ comments in a PR, the cumulative wait time could plausibly become perceptible as a pacing friction distinct from the adversarial content itself — a confound that bears directly on the interpretation of the 8-minute overhead threshold flagged in the analysis plan. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 2 — Reading the challenger response and typing a mandatory reply

Reaching the challenger message, a participant would plausibly experience two distinct cognitive demands sequentially: first parsing an unfamiliar code snippet from a different file, then constructing a written justification under the knowledge that the Approve button is locked. The cognitive load stacking here is meaningful, because the 20-character minimum creates a time pressure that could push participants toward the filler-reply failure mode the spec already anticipates, and the rate at which this happens is one of the most informative observable behaviors in the session. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant whose original comment was correct and whose confidence was appropriately high would likely experience the challenger as unhelpfully contrarian, because the counterexample-selection logic does not reliably distinguish "press harder on overconfidence" from "press on any confident comment." This concern would plausibly concentrate in the cases where the wizard applies the off-script fallback template ("What assumption about the calling context are you relying on here?") — a content-neutral prompt that, repeated often, could read as the AI being confused rather than rigorous. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The mandatory response box creates a design tension worth naming directly: it is simultaneously the mechanism by which the adversarial condition differs from the suggestion condition and the mechanism most likely to be gamed. A participant who has been gated twice in a row and is aware of the remaining hunks would reasonably discover that short plausible-sounding replies unlock the button just as effectively as substantive ones, and the rate of this discovery across the 20-minute PR window is a testable question rather than an assumption. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

- A rehearsal expectation is that the first gated challenge of the adversarial condition would produce the longest reply (in characters and in dwell time), with subsequent replies decaying in both length and substance as the participant adapts to the gate mechanic. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- A participant who values efficiency signals in their own team culture would plausibly read the response gate as paternalistic, which could contaminate the post-task helpfulness Likert item in ways that are hard to disentangle from genuine judgments about the AI's reasoning quality. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 3 — Approving, adjusting confidence, moving on

After submitting the mandatory reply, a participant facing the confidence slider again would likely either leave it unchanged (if they successfully dismissed the challenge mentally) or adjust it downward (if the challenge surfaced genuine uncertainty), and the direction is the primary calibration signal. A risk is that participants may not understand that they can revise the slider after the challenge, because the UI affordance for slider revision is not explicitly surfaced in the spec — a participant could reasonably interpret the post-reply state as "my comment is locked in" and move on without reconsidering their confidence, which would silently suppress the key outcome variable. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The per-PR Calibration Check questionnaire arriving immediately on review submission would plausibly be answered in a state of fatigue after 20 minutes of gated interactions, and item 5 ("How disruptive did you find the AI's interruptions?") would likely be elevated in the adversarial condition relative to suggestion — but this elevation could reflect either genuine disruption or residual annoyance from the mandatory gate, and these are not distinguishable from the questionnaire alone. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 4 — Cycling through three conditions with between-task measures

A participant completing the three conditions back-to-back would likely experience order effects that the Latin-square counterbalancing can balance at the group level but cannot remove from any individual session. The participant who encounters adversarial first would plausibly bring heightened scrutiny to the suggestion and no-tool conditions that follow, having been primed to look for counterexamples themselves, which is a contrast effect worth naming explicitly rather than treating as fully controlled by counterbalancing. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The NASA-TLX Mental Demand subscale as a single item between tasks would plausibly differentiate the adversarial condition from the others, but the interpretation of that difference is not obvious in advance: higher mental demand could reflect productive deliberation or it could reflect the cognitive cost of gaming the gate, and the questionnaire alone cannot distinguish these. Pairing this item with the wizard's substantive-vs-dismissive coding is the only path to disambiguation. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 5 — Post-session interview and debrief

The post-session interview questions as written would likely elicit more reflection on the adversarial condition than the other two, because the adversarial experience is more cognitively marked and therefore more available to recall. This asymmetry in recall is a plausible source of biased qualitative data that could overweight adversarial-condition themes in the interview corpus relative to their actual causal weight in the quantitative calibration measures. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The debrief revealing the Wizard-of-Oz nature of the AI responses would plausibly produce a range of reactions from mild surprise to active reinterpretation of the entire session, and a participant who feels they were deceived in a way they find uncomfortable could reasonably decline to have their data used — a consent re-check at debrief is not mentioned in the spec and may be worth adding to the rehearsal-to-protocol transition. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Friction hotspots

- The mandatory 20-character reply gate is the single highest-friction element in the entire session and would plausibly generate both the strongest adversarial-condition effects and the strongest failure-mode pressure; rehearsing this gate with a pilot participant before formal sessions begin is probably essential. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The wizard's real-time judgment of "substantive vs. dismissive" within a 3-second window for firing FOLLOW_UP_PROBE is a cognitively demanding task that would likely produce inconsistent coding across sessions and across wizards, and the inter-rater validation against audio-coded DISMISS episodes (targeted at >75% agreement) is the right check but may not be achievable without wizard calibration training prior to data collection. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The off-script fallback pathway (lightweight clarifying question when no counterexample fits) is functionally a third condition embedded inside the adversarial condition, and if it fires on more than a small minority of challenges, the adversarial condition becomes heterogeneous in ways that will complicate the Brier score comparison. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The 2–4 second simulated delay, repeated across 10–20 challenges per PR, would plausibly accumulate into a pacing experience that the participant attributes to "AI slowness" and that contaminates the helpfulness Likert in ways that are not about the content of the challenges at all. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The confidence slider's 1–5 granularity would likely produce clustering at 3 that limits the variance available for the Brier score analysis, particularly for participants who are slider-conservative by disposition. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Risks to interpretation

A participant who games the mandatory gate with filler replies would generate wizard log entries of "gate-bypassed" episodes, but the Brier score analysis does not currently specify whether these episodes are included, excluded, or treated as a moderator — and each choice produces a materially different inferential story. Pre-registering this decision before data collection is probably necessary to avoid analyst degrees of freedom in the primary comparison. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The confound between "mandatory gate friction" and "counterexample content" is structural to the design: the adversarial condition differs from the suggestion condition on both dimensions simultaneously. A participant's reduced Brier score in the adversarial condition could reflect improved calibration from engaging with counterexamples, or it could reflect improved calibration from the forced deliberation pause itself — and the current design cannot fully separate these. A fourth condition (suggestion content + mandatory response gate, or adversarial content without gate) would be the cleanest rehearsal-informed addition but would push session duration beyond feasibility. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's dual role as real-time judge (of comment correctness, confidence calibration, and reply substantiveness) and real-time operator (of the control panel) is a demanding cognitive load that would plausibly degrade over the course of a 75-minute active window, and late-session wizard decisions may be systematically different from early-session decisions in ways that interact with condition order. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The ground-truth answer keys for the three legacy PRs are themselves research-team constructions, and a participant who catches a real defect not in the key, or who correctly identifies a non-defect the key flagged, would be coded as wrong under the current analysis plan. The ecological legitimacy of the calibration comparison rests on the answer keys being genuinely correct, and a rehearsal-stage external code review of the answer keys by an uninvolved senior engineer would plausibly be valuable. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

- Do participants revise their confidence slider values after challenger responses at materially different rates than they revise them without challenges, or does slider revision behavior depend more on individual disposition than on condition? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- How often does the wizard fall back to the off-script clarifying-question template, and does the proportion of fallback episodes correlate with PR difficulty or participant comment-rate? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- What is the inter-wizard agreement rate on the substantive-vs-dismissive coding of the same participant reply, measured against a set of held-out transcripts before formal sessions begin? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Do participants interpret the mandatory response gate as a feature of the AI tool they are evaluating, or as a bug or design flaw — and does this interpretation shift as the session progresses? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Is the 2–4 second simulated processing delay read as plausible AI latency across all three PRs, or do participants show behavioral signs of noticing the delay as artificial, particularly when the wizard's timing under load slips outside the intended window? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Does the post-session interview recall of "moments I changed my mind" concentrate asymmetrically in the adversarial condition relative to what the slider-revision logs show, indicating a reconstructive memory effect rather than a direct report of in-session cognition? [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Among participants counterbalanced to encounter the adversarial condition first, does the behavior in the subsequent suggestion and no-tool conditions differ in detectable ways from participants who encountered those conditions first — that is, does exposure to adversarial challenge produce carryover effects that the Latin square cannot fully neutralize?
