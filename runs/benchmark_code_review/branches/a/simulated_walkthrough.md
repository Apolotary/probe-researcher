## Session opening

A participant arriving at this session would plausibly carry in two priors that could shape the first ten minutes disproportionately: a self-image as a competent reviewer (given the 2–6 years experience eligibility) and a generalized curiosity or wariness about AI coding tools that has become common in the professional population the study samples from. The facilitator's framing of the think-aloud protocol would likely feel familiar to some participants and awkward to others, and a rehearsal concern is that the consent language around "a code review web app" may not fully disguise the study's interest in the AI condition specifically — a participant who reads the consent form carefully might reasonably infer that AI assistance is the variable of interest, which would cue demand characteristics from the outset. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The warm-up PR on an unseeded repository is intended to acclimate the participant to Gitea, but a rehearsal question is whether five minutes is sufficient for a participant unfamiliar with Gitea specifically (as opposed to GitHub) to stop noticing interface differences. A participant who spends the warm-up still hunting for the "Files changed" tab would plausibly carry interface-orientation load into the first seeded PR, which could confound early dwell-time measures with tool-learning effects rather than review-strategy effects. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The background questionnaire item on repository familiarity would likely function well as a screen, but a rehearsal concern is that participants may under-report familiarity with repositories they have only skimmed or depended on transitively. A participant who has read source from a widely-used library without "contributing" might truthfully answer "no" while still bringing substantial convention knowledge to the session, which would erode the tribal-knowledge manipulation in ways the backup-PR mechanism cannot catch. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Task flow walkthrough

### Step 1: Opening the PR and initial orientation

A participant opening the seeded PR in the AI-assistant condition would likely have their attention drawn immediately to the "AI Review Assistant (Beta)" sidebar with its "analyzing the diff" spinner, even before they finish the PR description. This would plausibly create a subtle commitment effect: the participant forms an expectation that comments will arrive, and their initial scroll through the diff could become a waiting-for-the-AI scroll rather than an independent orientation scroll. A rehearsal concern is that dwell-time on File 1 during this step may be systematically inflated in the AI condition simply because participants are pacing themselves against the anticipated AI output. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's decision to wait until the participant has scrolled past the PR description before triggering the first comment block is well-motivated, but in practice the cue ("scrolling past it") is ambiguous because a participant may scroll past, then scroll back to reread, then scroll down again. A wizard watching this pattern would plausibly hesitate, and that hesitation could produce variable latencies between participants that correlate with reading style rather than with condition, injecting noise into time-to-first-AI-comment as a covariate. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

In the no-assistant condition, the absence of the sidebar would likely feel entirely normal to participants familiar with standard Gitea or GitHub, and orientation behavior in this step would plausibly be the cleanest signal the study captures. A rehearsal question is whether this cleanliness itself is a problem: the AI condition introduces a UI element that is not present in the control, so any attention-allocation difference could be driven by the mere presence of a sidebar competing for screen real estate, independent of its content. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 2: Inline commenting begins and AI comments stream in

The animation of AI comments "streaming" into the sidebar after File 1 would likely create a strong attention capture moment. A participant mid-way through formulating their own comment on a different line would plausibly experience a micro-interruption, and the friction point here is not whether they notice the comments but whether they finish their own thought first or switch to read the AI's output. A rehearsal concern is that the coding scheme for think-aloud utterances (categories 1–4) does not obviously capture this interruption event itself, which may be an important mediating process. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's SuppressComment rule — removing a pre-scripted AI comment when the participant has already posted a human comment on the same line — would reasonably preserve illusion but introduces a subtle selection effect: the AI's apparent "catch rate" will systematically under-represent overlap with fast, accurate reviewers. A participant who works quickly and catches local defects before the wizard triggers would see an AI that appears to flag fewer issues than it "actually" would have, and this participant's perceived-helpfulness Likert rating may therefore be driven by their own speed rather than by AI quality. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A rehearsal concern for this step is the illusion-maintenance cost of comments "always being of the local-checkable class." A sophisticated participant reading three AI comments — null-pointer, unused import, off-by-one — in succession might plausibly form a meta-hypothesis: "this AI only catches surface stuff." If that hypothesis forms in Block 1, it could alter behavior in Block 2 (either compensatory architectural scrutiny, or resigned dismissal of the AI entirely), and this carryover would confound the within-participant design more than the counterbalancing can absorb. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 3: Navigation to architectural and convention files

This step is the heart of the study, and a user encountering it would plausibly experience the most interesting friction. A participant who has seen the AI flag three local defects and then navigates to a later file containing an architectural-drift defect, only to see "No issues detected in this file," would likely interpret that placeholder as weak positive evidence that the file is clean. The friction here is invisible to the participant but is precisely the attention-shift the study is designed to detect — which means the primary dependent measure depends on participants not realizing what is happening to them. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant who habitually reviews by opening every file regardless of signals would plausibly show the attenuated version of this effect, while a participant who uses AI signals as a triage mechanism would show a pronounced effect. A rehearsal concern is that the sample of 10 participants is unlikely to be large enough to separate these two review-style populations, and the primary 2×4 repeated-measures comparison may wash them together in a way that obscures the mechanism rather than illuminating it. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard decision not to intervene if a participant appears about to exit the PR without visiting an architectural-drift file is principled but operationally difficult. A wizard watching a participant hover near the "Submit Review" button would plausibly feel tempted to re-inspect the file list to confirm attrition is real rather than a misclick, and this active monitoring could produce small timing artifacts in when InjectCountSummary fires. A rehearsal question is whether the wizard log's "arrived at arch-file" timestamps will be reliable enough for downstream alignment, given that file-navigation events in Gitea are not always cleanly bounded. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 4: Review submission and count summary

The "AI flagged 3–5 issues, you flagged N issues" summary would plausibly land very differently on different participants. A participant whose N is lower than the AI count might reasonably experience a mild confidence hit — interpreting the gap as evidence they missed something — and their Likert response on "perceived review completeness" could shift downward in ways that mask the real effect the study is testing (whether AI-condition completeness ratings are inflated relative to actual detection). A rehearsal concern is that this summary screen itself is an experimental manipulation embedded inside what is ostensibly a measurement step. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant whose N is higher than the AI count would plausibly experience the opposite: a small confidence boost from "beating" the AI, which could inflate the completeness rating in the AI-assisted condition in a direction opposite to the study's hypothesis. The net effect across participants is indeterminate in rehearsal, and a design question worth flagging is whether the count summary should be shown at all before the post-session questionnaire, since its presence couples two measurements that might better be kept independent. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The accidental-click undo path (wizard IMs facilitator if submit happens within 3 seconds of "wait") would plausibly fire rarely, but when it fires it introduces a three-way communication channel under time pressure — wizard to facilitator to participant — and a rehearsal concern is that this path has not been drilled enough for the facilitator to deliver the undo offer in a naturalistic voice rather than a scripted one. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 5: Post-session questionnaire, interview, and debrief

By the time a participant reaches the questionnaire, they would likely have been reviewing code for close to 100 minutes with only a brief mid-session break, and fatigue effects on the open-ended responses would plausibly be substantial. A participant asked to write five open-ended responses after four PR reviews might reasonably produce shorter, less reflective answers than the codebook assumes, and the kappa ≥ 0.70 target on AI-deference coding may be harder to achieve on sparse text than on rich text. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The retrospective interview questions Q4 ("Did the AI assistant change what you paid attention to?") and Q6 ("How much did you trust the 'no issues detected' message?") would plausibly elicit post-hoc rationalization more than accurate recall, because participants at this point have been reviewing four PRs and would likely blend memories across trials. A rehearsal concern is that the interview data may be most useful as a source of hypotheses about individual differences rather than as evidence of the attention-shift mechanism itself. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The WoZ debrief at session end would plausibly produce a mix of reactions — amusement, mild betrayal, intellectual curiosity — and a rehearsal question is whether the debrief script gives participants enough space to register negative reactions without social pressure to perform acceptance. A participant who feels deceived but does not say so could carry that reaction into future studies the lab runs, which is a longer-horizon cost worth noting. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Friction hotspots

- The moment AI comments stream in after File 1 is likely the single highest-variance event in the session: attention capture, interruption to ongoing reasoning, and formation of meta-hypotheses about AI capability would all plausibly concentrate here, and current measurement does not cleanly separate them. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The "No issues detected in this file" placeholder on architectural-drift files is the designed manipulation, but it depends on the placeholder being salient enough to influence triage decisions while not being salient enough to prompt suspicion — a narrow target that would plausibly land differently across participants. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The count summary on submission couples an experimental manipulation with a measurement step and could plausibly contaminate the completeness Likert subscale in either direction depending on the participant's N relative to the AI's count. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The think-aloud protocol would plausibly degrade over a 120-minute session in ways that are not linear: silence would likely cluster in PRs 3 and 4, and the "three prompts = low-verbalization flag" rule may systematically exclude precisely the participants who are most cognitively loaded, which is a non-random missingness pattern. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The counterbalancing of 2 conditions × 4 PRs across 10 participants is tight, and a single manipulation-failed trial (e.g., WebSocket drop) would plausibly unbalance cells enough to destabilize the repeated-measures analysis. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Risks to interpretation

A primary risk is that the AI-condition sidebar introduces a visual element absent from the control condition, so any attention-allocation difference is ambiguously attributable to the sidebar's content (what the AI said) versus its mere presence (a UI element competing for gaze). A rehearsal concern is that a cleaner control would include a placeholder sidebar in the no-assistant condition — perhaps labeled "AI Review Assistant (Disabled)" — to isolate content effects from presence effects, and the absence of this control means the study as designed can detect an attention shift but cannot strongly attribute it to AI content specifically. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A second risk is carryover between blocks. A participant who forms a meta-hypothesis about the AI's limitations in Block 1 would plausibly review differently in Block 2 regardless of condition, and counterbalancing order cannot fully absorb this because the hypothesis itself is condition-triggered, not order-triggered. The study design would likely benefit from a between-subjects comparison as a sensitivity check, though the sample size does not support this. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A third risk is ecological validity of the 20-minute cap. Real PR reviews in industry settings vary widely in duration, and imposing a uniform cap with a 17-minute warning banner would plausibly compress review behavior toward a time-constrained triage style that exaggerates reliance on any available signal, including AI signals. The observed attention shift in the study may therefore overstate the effect size that would obtain in untimed reviews. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A fourth risk is that the seeded defects, being seeded, may read differently from organic defects. A participant who senses something "planted" about the code — an unusually clean service-layer violation, say — would plausibly shift into test-taking mode rather than reviewer mode, and this shift would affect detection rates in ways that do not reflect natural review behavior. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
