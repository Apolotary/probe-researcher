## Session opening

At the start of a given weekly session, a participant joining the Zoom would plausibly need a moment to orient themselves to the layered nature of the setup — their own VS Code window, the Chrome tab pointing at localhost:4000, the screen-share indicator, and the awareness that an unseen observer and wizard are watching. For a mid-level engineer unfamiliar with the study codebase, this multi-surface arrangement could reasonably produce a low-grade cognitive overhead in the opening minutes, particularly in week 1 before the ritual is internalized. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The facilitator's scripted brief naming the week's focus area (e.g., "Active Record callbacks and their interactions with the transaction layer") would likely land differently across weeks: in early sessions, a participant might not yet have the vocabulary to anchor "callbacks" and "transaction layer" to concrete files, whereas by week 8 the same phrase could be expected to trigger specific file-path recall. This asymmetry means the session opening is not a constant — it is itself a longitudinal signal that the protocol does not currently instrument. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A treatment participant would plausibly enter the session with an expectancy about annotations that a control participant does not hold, because the treatment condition has conditioned them to await the Step 2 scaffold. This expectancy could reasonably begin shaping behavior before Step 2 — for example, a participant might navigate in Step 1 with one eye on "what the AI will probably tell me anyway," which would compress the independence assumption the study relies on. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Task flow walkthrough

### Step 1 — Navigation

A participant asked to "find the entry point for transaction rollback callbacks" using only VS Code global search and the file tree would likely begin with a keyword search like "rollback" or "callback," producing a wide hit list across the Rails monolith. For someone unfamiliar with the codebase, the 15-minute window could reasonably feel short given the size of the repository, and the think-aloud audio might concentrate uncertainty markers in the first 3–5 minutes as the participant triages which hits look promising. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's stuck-flag threshold (no file opened for 2+ minutes plus audible distress) would plausibly produce edge cases in interpretation — a participant who is reading a long file quietly is not stuck, but the wizard cannot always distinguish "reading deeply" from "frozen." This means the flag_stuck_participant control would likely be triggered with some noise, and sessions in which the scripted hint is offered would be expected to cluster in early weeks rather than spread uniformly. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A subtle friction point here is that treatment participants have the fixture PR interface available but are told annotations are "reserved for PR-submission moments." A curious participant might reasonably try to open the PR interface out of turn, or glance at it mid-navigation, which the protocol does not explicitly address beyond wizard logging. The boundary between "available" and "not-yet-active" is a design seam that would plausibly produce inconsistent participant interpretations. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 2 — PR submission and annotation

When a treatment participant submits the draft PR and begins "narrating their expectations aloud," the 60-to-90-second waiting window would likely feel long in real time, particularly without a visible loading indicator. A participant could reasonably interpret silence as the system "thinking," as a rejection of their PR description, or as a technical failure — and the think-aloud content during this window would plausibly be some of the richest data in the session for surfacing mental models of what the participant believes the AI is doing. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The wizard's decision between variant A and variant B hinges on identifying "a factually incorrect claim about which module is affected." This determination would likely be harder in practice than on paper, because PR descriptions often contain ambiguously-phrased claims rather than cleanly right-or-wrong assertions. Two wizards working independently could reasonably disagree on whether a given description warrants variant B, and without a calibration exercise between the two wizards, this inter-wizard drift would plausibly be a quiet source of protocol variance. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant reading the three-section annotation — historical commits, related modules, convention rationale — would reasonably spend different amounts of time on each section depending on their orientation. An engineer who defaults to git-archaeology might fixate on the commit SHAs; one who defaults to architectural reasoning might skim past commits to the related-modules section. This means annotation "consumption" is itself patterned, and the protocol's current capture (did the annotation appear, was it variant A or B) would plausibly under-represent what the participant actually absorbed. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The preface "AI Learning Scaffold (for your reference — not a blocking review)" is doing a lot of framing work. A participant might reasonably read this as permission to ignore the annotation, or alternatively as implicit authority ("the AI has looked at this and thinks these things matter"). These two readings would produce very different downstream behaviors in Steps 3 and 4, and they are not separated by any instrument the protocol currently includes. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 3 — Architectural sketching

Asked to draw at least 8 directional arrows on a Miro board of 20 labeled modules within 20 minutes, a participant in early weeks would plausibly struggle to reach the minimum count without either guessing or reaching for recently-seen material. The minimum-arrow requirement is a productive forcing function, but it also means the first several arrows under pressure would likely come from the most recently activated memory — which for treatment participants is the Step 2 annotation. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The failure case anticipating "annotation recall — direct copy observed" is well-motivated, but the detection mechanism depends on the participant verbalizing attribution ("the AI told me that module X connects to module Y"). A participant who silently copies annotation content without attributing it would plausibly go undetected, producing sketch data coded as "independently derived" when it is not. This asymmetry between verbalized and silent annotation influence would reasonably bias the mental-model accuracy measure upward for treatment participants in a way the protocol does not currently catch. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The under-4-arrows-at-10-minutes trigger and its scripted prompt ("no wrong answers — place arrows for relationships you are uncertain about too") would likely push a hesitant participant into producing low-confidence arrows. These arrows would plausibly score poorly against the 15-canonical-relationship answer key, which means the intervention designed to keep the participant engaged could simultaneously depress the accuracy signal for that session — a tension worth naming in the analysis plan. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 4 — Predictive debugging

A participant reading the bug-report PDF and being asked to name a file path and line range before opening any code is being asked to perform a compact mental-model probe. For a treatment participant whose Step 2 annotation happened to name the correct module, the "prediction" would reasonably be heavily annotation-influenced — and the wizard's "annotation-influenced prediction" flag is the right instrument for this, but its validity depends on the annotation sets being roughly balanced in how often they name fault-relevant modules across weeks. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The immediate feedback moment — facilitator opening VS Code to the correct location and briefly sharing the screen — would plausibly be emotionally loaded. A participant who predicted correctly might be expected to show visible relief; one who missed entirely could reasonably experience a confidence dip that carries into the Step 5 Likert scales administered minutes later. This feedback-to-Likert proximity means the Codebase Confidence scale is not measuring a stable trait — it is measuring a state partly set by the immediately-prior correctness revelation. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The failure case involving the control-group participant finding the bug in the PDF highlights a deeper issue: the predictive-debugging task's validity depends on neither group having a direct shortcut. For treatment, the shortcut is the annotation naming the fault module; for control, it is the documentation containing the documented issue. The protocol addresses the control shortcut but does not symmetrically address the treatment shortcut, which would plausibly be a reviewer-attracting asymmetry. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 5 — Interview, Likerts, reflection

By the time a participant reaches Step 5 at minute 70 of a 90-minute session, fatigue would plausibly compress responses on the three Likert instruments, particularly in later weeks when the scales become familiar and satisficing risk rises. A participant who has completed the AI Reliance Scale nine times before could reasonably respond from memory of their prior pattern rather than from the current session's experience. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The open-ended reflection prompt ("Describe in 2–3 sentences how your understanding of this area of the codebase changed during today's session, if at all") with a 100-character minimum is a thoughtful instrument, but the four-category coding scheme (breadth expansion / depth consolidation / confusion or regression / no change) imposes mutually-exclusive boundaries that real reflections would likely cross. A participant could plausibly describe both breadth expansion and confusion in the same reflection, and the forced-choice coding would reasonably lose this compound signal. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The Probe 6b follow-up ("were you recalling something specific or reasoning from the code") asks a participant to introspect accurately on the source of their earlier Miro arrows. Self-report on cognitive provenance is notoriously unreliable, and a participant might reasonably confabulate a "reasoning from the code" narrative for arrows that were in fact annotation-echoes, because the latter answer feels more effortful and engaged. This means the coding of "annotation-derived" versus "independently derived" is partly measuring participant willingness to admit dependence. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Friction hotspots

- The 60-to-90-second annotation wait in Step 2 is a design-critical moment: a participant's interpretation of silence during this window would plausibly shape their stance toward the annotation when it arrives, and the protocol currently instruments the annotation's appearance but not the participant's expectancy construction during the wait. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The boundary between "Step 1 navigation, PR interface visible but annotations reserved" and "Step 2, annotations active" is a behavioral seam where treatment participants would reasonably develop inconsistent interpretations across weeks. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Inter-wizard calibration on variant A versus variant B selection is a likely source of unacknowledged variance, particularly because the two wizards do not appear to have a cross-coding exercise before study launch. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The Miro minimum-arrow requirement and the under-4-arrows prompt together create pressure that would plausibly push low-confidence treatment participants toward recently-seen annotation content, which is the exact behavior the study wants to distinguish from genuine mental-model formation. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The proximity of the Step 4 correctness reveal to the Step 5 confidence Likert means the Codebase Confidence measure is partly a state measurement rather than a trait measurement, which would reasonably complicate the longitudinal trajectory analysis. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Silent annotation-copying in Step 3 (as opposed to verbalized annotation recall) would likely go undetected by the current observer-flag mechanism, producing a systematic under-count of annotation influence. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- A 90-minute session administered weekly for 8–12 weeks introduces participant-attrition and scale-fatigue risks that the protocol does not currently instrument — longitudinal engagement itself is an uncontrolled variable. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Risks to interpretation

The treatment-versus-control comparison rests on the assumption that the two conditions differ only in the presence of the annotation scaffold, but the control condition (documentation PDF plus VS Code search) and the treatment condition (fixture PR interface plus annotations plus documentation PDF is unavailable to treatment per the spec's Part 2 description) differ in multiple dimensions simultaneously — presence of scaffold, presence of documentation, and the interactional frame of "submitting a PR." This multi-dimensional difference would reasonably make it difficult to attribute any observed effect specifically to the AI scaffold rather than to the PR-submission ritual itself. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The annotation library's 12 pre-authored sets, selected by the wizard on a "closest match even if not exact" rule, introduces a mismatch variance that is logged but not controlled. Sessions in which the annotation matched poorly would plausibly produce different participant behaviors than sessions with tight matches, and the analysis plan does not currently treat annotation-fit quality as a moderator variable. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The longitudinal design across 8–12 weeks assumes that participants remain comparable across time, but real-world confounds — participants doing work in other codebases between sessions, reading Rails material on their own, changing jobs — would reasonably accumulate. The study captures in-session behavior richly but has no instrument for between-session exposure, which means week-over-week trajectories are partly measuring uncontrolled external learning. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The sketch-accuracy answer key (15 canonical relationships signed off by two senior Rails engineers) is itself a single source of ground truth. If the two engineers agree systematically on a particular architectural framing that other senior engineers would not share, the "accuracy" measure is partly measuring alignment-with-rubric rather than architectural correctness in some stricter sense. This would reasonably be worth stress-testing with a third reviewer before study launch. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
