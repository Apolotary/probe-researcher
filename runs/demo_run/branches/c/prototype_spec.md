# Prototype: Longitudinal WoZ Co-Design Study: In-Browser Accessibility Repair Overlay for BLV Screen-Reader Users Navigating AI-Generated Articles

## Context

- **Setting:** A quiet, single-occupancy usability lab room at the research institution. The participant sits at a desk with their assigned device. The wizard sits in an adjacent room connected via one-way audio and a mirrored screen feed. The observer sits behind the participant (approximately 1.5 m away) and does not speak. All sessions are conducted in-person. Three sessions per participant are scheduled across three consecutive weeks (Sessions 1, 2, 3), with at least 5 days between sessions to allow co-design revisions to the overlay configuration.
- **Device:** Windows 11 desktop, Chrome 130 at 1440x900 resolution, NVDA 2024.1 screen reader with default speech rate (180 wpm) and default verbosity; participants who use VoiceOver on macOS may bring their own MacBook (macOS 14, Safari 17) but must disable any personal browser extensions for the session duration.
- **Duration:** 90 minutes

Session 1 (90 min): 10 min onboarding and consent review; 5 min device orientation and screen-reader settings check; 30 min baseline navigation tasks on 4 AI-generated articles with repair overlay disabled (wizard observes only); 15 min think-aloud debrief and Likert ratings; 20 min co-design discussion — participant annotates problem moments on printed article DOM tree diagrams and proposes repair rules verbally; 10 min facilitator summarises agreed repair rules and reads them back to participant for confirmation. Sessions 2 and 3 (90 min each): 5 min recap of previous session findings; 30 min navigation tasks on 4 new AI-generated articles with repair overlay active (wizard operating in real time per agreed rules); 15 min think-aloud debrief and Likert ratings; 20 min co-design refinement — participant reviews screen-reader speech log excerpts and proposes rule modifications; 10 min facilitator reads back revised ruleset for confirmation. Final 10 min of Session 3: participant completes a structured retrospective interview covering which failure categories the overlay resolved versus those that persisted.

## Actors

- **participant** (6): A blind or low-vision adult who uses a screen reader (NVDA on Windows or VoiceOver on macOS) as their primary web-browsing tool, recruited with at least 2 years of daily screen-reader navigation experience and no prior exposure to the repair overlay prototype.
- **wizard** (1): A researcher fluent in HTML/DOM manipulation who monitors the participant's screen-reader output via a mirrored display and operates the repair overlay control panel in real time, deciding when and how to trigger heading rewrites and landmark restructuring before the screen reader announces them.
- **observer** (1): A silent researcher who logs timestamped navigation events, screen-reader speech output, and participant verbal reactions in a structured observation sheet, without intervening in the session.
- **facilitator** (1): A researcher who briefs the participant, administers tasks, prompts think-aloud narration, conducts the post-session co-design debrief, and collects Likert and open-ended ratings between tasks — this role is distinct from the wizard and does not touch the control panel.

## Task flow

### Step 1

- **Participant:** Participant navigates to a pre-loaded AI-generated news article (loaded in Chrome from a local HTML fixture file, URL displayed as 'article1.local') using only keyboard and screen reader. Participant is instructed to: first use the screen reader's heading-navigation shortcut (H key in NVDA, or VO+Command+H in VoiceOver) to build a mental map of the article structure, then locate and read aloud the third section heading, then navigate to the final paragraph of the article.
- **System:** In Session 1 (baseline), the page loads with the raw AI-generated HTML — no overlay intervention. The screen reader announces headings and landmarks exactly as they appear in the source DOM. In Sessions 2 and 3, the overlay JavaScript is injected at page load; the wizard may trigger DOM rewrites before or during navigation, and the screen reader announces the rewritten structure.
- **Wizard decisions:**
  - Decide whether the article's heading hierarchy contains detectable hallucinated headings (e.g., an H2 that is semantically a continuation of body text rather than a new section) and flag them in the wizard control panel before the participant begins heading navigation.
  - Decide whether to pre-apply all flagged repairs at page load ('batch mode') or to hold repairs and apply them reactively as the participant encounters each heading, based on the co-designed ruleset from the prior session.
  - Decide whether landmark nesting anomalies (e.g., a <nav> inside an <article> with no label) should be repaired silently or replaced with an ARIA label announcement — the default is silent repair unless the participant's Session 1 co-design notes indicated they want audible confirmation of repairs.

### Step 2

- **Participant:** After completing heading navigation, the participant attempts to locate a specific named subsection — the facilitator reads aloud a target phrase (e.g., 'Find the section discussing policy implications') — using any navigation strategy the participant chooses (headings, landmarks, search, or linear reading). Participant narrates their strategy continuously via think-aloud.
- **System:** The overlay's landmark-repair module monitors which ARIA landmark the screen reader is currently inside (tracked via a MutationObserver logging postMessage events to the wizard panel). If the participant enters a landmark region whose label the wizard has flagged as ambiguous or absent, the wizard may trigger an ARIA-label injection mid-navigation, which the screen reader will announce at the next focus event or when the participant re-enters the region.
- **Wizard decisions:**
  - Decide whether the participant's current navigation path indicates they are disoriented (operationally defined as: three or more consecutive H key presses without a 'found it' or equivalent verbal signal, or explicit verbal expression of confusion) — if so, decide whether to trigger the 'landmark-label inject' control or to withhold and observe whether the participant self-recovers.
  - Decide whether an injected ARIA label should use the wizard's own descriptive text or the repair rule text co-designed with the participant in the previous session; default to co-designed text when available.
  - Decide whether to log this navigation path as a 'repair-resistant failure' if the participant does not locate the target within 3 minutes despite overlay activation, and note the specific DOM feature that appears to be causing breakdown.

### Step 3

- **Participant:** Participant completes a structured post-article rating task: using keyboard-navigable on-screen forms (pre-built HTML fixture), they rate on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = completely disoriented, 5 = completely oriented) their sense of structural orientation during heading navigation and during subsection search, then answer two open-ended text fields: 'Describe the moment, if any, when the page structure felt wrong or unexpected' and 'Describe anything the page did that helped you orient yourself.'
- **System:** The rating form is a static HTML fixture with no overlay active. Screen reader announces form labels and input types normally. The observer timestamps the moment the participant submits the form. The wizard saves the DOM-repair event log for this article to a per-participant, per-session log file named [participantID]_session[N]_article[M]_repair_log.json.
- **Wizard decisions:**
  - Decide whether to add a debrief note to the repair log flagging any wizard decision during this article where the wizard was uncertain whether to intervene — these uncertain-decision moments will be surfaced in the co-design debrief.
  - Decide whether to show the participant a visual diff of the original vs. repaired DOM (rendered as an indented plain-text tree read aloud by the screen reader) during the post-article debrief, or to describe changes verbally via the facilitator — the default is verbal description unless the participant requested DOM-tree review in a prior session.

### Step 4

- **Participant:** After all 4 articles in a session are complete, participant joins a structured co-design debrief. The facilitator presents three to five timestamped screen-reader speech excerpts (selected by the observer from the session log) where navigation breakdowns occurred. For each excerpt, the participant is asked: 'What did you hear, what did you expect to hear, and what rule change — if any — would have helped?' Participant responses are audio-recorded and the facilitator writes proposed rule changes on a shared rule-card template (a printed card with fields: Trigger condition / Repair action / Confidence threshold).
- **System:** No live overlay is active during debrief. The facilitator's laptop displays a static HTML rendering of the rule-card template that the wizard can read on the mirrored feed. After the debrief, the wizard transcribes agreed rule changes into the overlay's configuration JSON file (repair_rules_[participantID]_session[N].json) within 48 hours, ready for the next session.
- **Wizard decisions:**
  - Decide whether a participant-proposed repair rule is technically implementable within the overlay's DOM-rewriting capabilities (heading tag substitution, ARIA role/label injection, nesting flattening) or requires a fallback — if not implementable, the wizard flags this for the facilitator to disclose to the participant at the start of the next session.
  - Decide whether two proposed rules conflict (e.g., participant wants H3 tags always relabelled as H2, but a prior rule preserves H3 for lists of three or fewer items) and draft a resolution to present to the participant at the next session's recap.

### Step 5

- **Participant:** At the end of Session 3 only, participant completes a 10-minute retrospective interview conducted by the facilitator. Questions are: (1) 'Across the three sessions, which types of navigation problem became easier with the overlay?', (2) 'Which types of problem persisted even when the overlay was active?', (3) 'Were there any moments when the overlay made navigation harder or introduced new confusion?', (4) 'If you could change one thing about how the overlay works, what would it be?' Responses are audio-recorded.
- **System:** No system is active. The facilitator takes structured notes on a printed interview guide. The observer timestamps each question transition.
- **Wizard decisions:**
  - Decide whether to share the aggregate repair-event log summary (total repairs triggered per session, repair types, and wizard-flagged uncertain-decision moments) with the participant during this interview as a memory aid — the default is to share a plain-text summary read aloud by the facilitator.
  - Decide whether any participant responses to question (2) (persistent failures) match the pre-specified 'repair-resistant failure' categories logged during sessions, and note mismatches for post-hoc analysis.

## Wizard controls

- **heading_rewrite_trigger** — triggers: Wizard activates this control when the participant's screen reader is about to announce a heading that the wizard has pre-flagged as hallucinated or structurally misleading — specifically, when a heading tag's text content is semantically a continuation of the preceding paragraph rather than a new section title, or when the heading level skips more than one level (e.g., H2 followed immediately by H5 with no intervening H3 or H4). The wizard must activate this control before the screen reader announces the heading, meaning the wizard must anticipate navigation by monitoring the participant's H-key press rate and current DOM position shown on the mirrored feed.. Effect: Injects a JavaScript DOM mutation that replaces the flagged heading tag with the correct heading level and, if the heading text is ambiguous, substitutes the text with the co-designed replacement label stored in repair_rules_[participantID]_session[N].json. The mutation fires within 200 ms of wizard activation. The screen reader announces the corrected heading text and level at the next focus or virtual-cursor movement. A timestamped entry is written to the repair log: {type: 'heading_rewrite', original_tag, original_text, replacement_tag, replacement_text, timestamp_ms}.
- **landmark_label_inject** — triggers: Wizard activates this control when the participant enters a landmark region (detected via postMessage from the page's MutationObserver) that has no ARIA label or has an ARIA label the wizard flagged as ambiguous during pre-session article review, AND the participant's think-aloud narration or navigation behaviour (three or more repeated landmark-navigation keystrokes — F6 in NVDA or VO+Right in VoiceOver — without a 'found it' signal) indicates disorientation within that region.. Effect: Injects an aria-label attribute onto the landmark element using the text specified in the current session's repair_rules JSON for that landmark type. If no rule exists, the wizard types a free-text label into a text input on the control panel and presses Enter to inject it. The screen reader announces the label the next time the participant enters or re-focuses the landmark. A timestamped entry is written to the repair log: {type: 'landmark_label_inject', landmark_role, injected_label, was_rule_based (boolean), timestamp_ms}.
- **nesting_flatten_trigger** — triggers: Wizard activates this control when the page's heading outline (shown in the wizard panel as a live-updating indented list generated by a background DOM walker) displays a nesting anomaly that the participant is approaching — specifically, a heading sequence where three or more consecutive headings are at the same level but their text content implies a parent-child relationship, causing the screen reader to announce them as peers rather than hierarchy.. Effect: Executes a pre-scripted DOM rewrite that promotes or demotes the affected heading tags to reflect the implied hierarchy, based on the rule set co-designed with the participant. The rewrite is applied to all affected headings simultaneously (batch, not one at a time) so the outline is consistent before the participant navigates through the section. A timestamped entry is written to the repair log: {type: 'nesting_flatten', affected_heading_ids (array), original_levels (array), replacement_levels (array), timestamp_ms}.
- **repair_hold** — triggers: Wizard activates this control at any point to suppress all automatic overlay mutations for 60 seconds — used when the participant appears to be navigating successfully without overlay assistance, or when the facilitator signals (via a pre-agreed hand gesture visible on the lab camera) that the participant is in a think-aloud verbalization that should not be interrupted by DOM changes.. Effect: Sets an overlay flag `repairs_suppressed = true` for 60 seconds, during which no DOM mutations fire even if the wizard presses other control buttons. A visual countdown timer displays on the wizard panel. After 60 seconds, the flag resets to false automatically. A log entry records {type: 'repair_hold', duration_ms: 60000, triggered_by: 'wizard_manual', timestamp_ms}.

## Observable signals

- **Screen-reader speech output stream — the full sequence of strings announced by NVDA or VoiceOver during each article navigation task, timestamped to the millisecond.** (screen_recording) — After each session, the observer exports the screen recording's audio channel and runs it through a speech-to-text transcription (Whisper large-v3, local deployment). The resulting transcript is segmented by article and by navigation phase (heading scan, subsection search, linear reading). Each segment is coded for the presence of the following event types: (a) heading level announcement followed within 5 seconds by a repeated heading-navigation keystroke (operationally defined as a 'heading-disorientation event'), (b) landmark announcement with no label (operationally defined as 'unlabelled-landmark encounter'), (c) screen reader announcing 'blank' or repeating the same heading text twice in succession (operationally defined as 'structural loop'). Event counts per article and per session are tabulated in a per-participant matrix. Across sessions, the change in event-type counts from Session 1 (baseline, no overlay) to Sessions 2 and 3 (overlay active) is computed to identify which event types decreased, which persisted, and which appeared only in overlay-active sessions (potential overlay-introduced confusions).
- **Participant think-aloud narration — verbal expressions of expectation, surprise, confusion, or satisfaction during navigation, timestamped against the screen-reader speech stream.** (think_aloud_audio) — The observer's structured observation sheet uses a pre-defined coding vocabulary with 6 categories: EXPECT (participant states what they anticipate before navigating), CONFIRM (participant states structure matched expectation), DISCONFIRM (participant states structure violated expectation), RECOVER (participant states they found a workaround), ABANDON (participant stops attempting the target task and says so), and REPAIR-NOTICE (participant explicitly notices or comments on an overlay-induced change). After each session, the facilitator and observer independently apply these codes to the transcribed think-aloud audio in 30-second windows. Inter-rater disagreements are resolved by reviewing the corresponding screen-recording segment. Coded event frequencies are compared across sessions to track whether DISCONFIRM and ABANDON rates change with overlay activation, and whether REPAIR-NOTICE events correlate with wizard log entries for overlay triggers.
- **Task completion time per article — the elapsed time from the moment the screen reader announces the page title to the moment the participant verbally confirms they have located the target subsection or declares they cannot find it.** (task_completion_time) — The observer records start and end timestamps on the structured observation sheet for each of the 4 articles per session. Times are tabulated per participant per session. Because articles differ across sessions (different AI-generated content), absolute time comparisons are not made. Instead, within-session variability (SD of completion times across 4 articles) is computed as a proxy for overlay consistency: a lower SD in Sessions 2 and 3 relative to Session 1 would indicate the overlay is normalising navigation difficulty across structurally variable articles. Articles in Sessions 2 and 3 where completion time exceeds the Session 1 mean by more than 1.5x are flagged as overlay-resistant cases and cross-referenced with the repair log to identify which repair types were active.
- **Wizard repair-event log — a machine-readable JSON record of every DOM mutation triggered by the wizard, including control name, timestamp, article ID, heading or landmark affected, and whether the repair was rule-based or free-text.** (log_events) — After all three sessions for a participant are complete, the repair logs are aggregated into a per-participant repair-event table. Each event is joined to the screen-reader speech transcript by timestamp (±500 ms window) to determine whether the repair fired before, during, or after the participant encountered the affected element. Events where the repair fired after the participant's disorientation event (wizard reacted too late) are tagged 'late repair' and excluded from efficacy analysis. For each repair type (heading_rewrite, landmark_label_inject, nesting_flatten), the proportion of events that were followed within 10 seconds by a CONFIRM think-aloud code (or absence of DISCONFIRM) is computed as a per-repair-type 'apparent resolution rate.' This rate is compared across sessions to detect learning effects in wizard decision-making and to identify repair types with persistently low resolution rates.
- **Post-article structural orientation Likert ratings — participant's self-reported sense of orientation (1–5 scale) immediately after completing each article's navigation tasks.** (self_report_likert) — Ratings are entered by the participant via the keyboard-navigable HTML rating form and stored as form-submission events logged to a local JSON file. Four ratings per session (one per article) yield 12 total ratings per participant across 3 sessions. Ratings are plotted per participant as a session-by-session trajectory. Because the study is longitudinal co-design (not an RCT), no inferential statistics are computed; instead, individual trajectories are described and any participant whose Session 3 ratings are lower than Session 1 (suggesting overlay introduced new disorientation) is flagged for detailed repair-log and think-aloud review. Ratings for the four articles within each session are also compared to identify within-session article difficulty variance.
- **Co-design debrief proposed repair rules — the specific rule changes proposed by each participant after each session, as recorded on the rule-card template.** (self_report_open_ended) — After the study, all rule-card templates across all participants and sessions are transcribed. Each proposed rule is coded against three dimensions: (1) Repair type targeted (heading level, heading text, landmark label, nesting structure, or other); (2) Trigger specificity (general, e.g., 'always fix skipped levels' vs. article-specific, e.g., 'fix only in news articles with AI byline'); (3) Implementability status as assessed by the wizard (implemented as-is, implemented with modification, not implementable). Rules that were not implementable are analysed as a set to characterise the limits of post-hoc DOM repair — these form the basis for answering the research question's sub-claim about 'limits of post-hoc structural correction.'

## Failure cases

- **The wizard triggers heading_rewrite_trigger after the screen reader has already begun announcing the original heading text — because NVDA begins speech as soon as the virtual cursor lands on the element, and the DOM mutation fires 200 ms after wizard activation, the participant hears the original (hallucinated) heading label followed immediately by the corrected label as the screen reader re-reads the mutated element, resulting in two conflicting announcements for the same heading.** — expected: The participant will likely pause, express confusion ('it just said two different things'), and may re-navigate to the heading to hear it again, potentially triggering additional speech output. The observer codes this as a DISCONFIRM event; the repair log records the event as a 'late repair.'. Wizard fallback: If the wizard recognises the double-announcement has occurred (visible in the screen-reader speech stream on the mirrored feed), they immediately activate repair_hold for 60 seconds to suppress further mutations while the participant re-orients. The facilitator does not intervene verbally. After the participant resumes navigation, the wizard notes the specific heading in the debrief log as a 'timing failure' candidate and proposes to the participant in the co-design debrief that this heading type should be handled via pre-load batch repair (heading_rewrite applied before page is announced) rather than reactive repair in future sessions.
- **The AI-generated article fixture contains a heading whose hallucination is semantic rather than structural — the heading tag level is correct (e.g., H3 under H2) but the heading text names a concept that contradicts the body text beneath it (an AI confabulation), so the overlay's DOM-based detection heuristics (which check tag levels and ARIA roles, not semantic content) do not flag it, and the wizard also fails to notice it during pre-session article review because the confabulation requires domain knowledge to detect.** — expected: The participant navigates to the heading, reads or listens to the body text beneath it, and encounters a semantic mismatch that the overlay does not repair. The participant may express confusion or attribute the disorientation to their own comprehension failure rather than to document structure. The screen-reader speech stream and think-aloud will not show a heading-disorientation event (the heading level was correct), so this failure is invisible to the quantitative coding scheme.. Wizard fallback: The wizard cannot repair semantic confabulation in real time with the current overlay architecture (which operates on tag structure and ARIA attributes, not text semantics). The wizard logs this article section as a 'content-semantic failure, not structurally detectable' in the repair log using a manual annotation field. In the post-session co-design debrief, the facilitator surfaces the specific excerpt and asks the participant whether they noticed any mismatch — if the participant did notice it, the event is recoded as a detected-but-unrepaired failure. This failure type is reported in the study as a category of breakdown that lies outside the scope of DOM-layer repair, directly addressing the research question's sub-claim about repair limits.
- **The participant's screen reader (NVDA with specific verbosity settings or a non-default punctuation level) announces landmark roles or heading levels differently from what the wizard's monitoring setup anticipated — for example, the wizard's mirrored display shows the DOM structure but the participant's NVDA verbosity setting suppresses landmark announcements entirely, meaning the wizard triggers landmark_label_inject controls that the participant never hears.** — expected: The participant continues to navigate without receiving the injected label announcement. The repair log records the injection as fired, but the participant's think-aloud shows no REPAIR-NOTICE event. The wizard may interpret the absence of disorientation as successful repair, when in fact the repair was silently voided by screen-reader settings.. Wizard fallback: At the start of each session, the facilitator runs a 5-minute device check that includes a standardised NVDA settings verification protocol: the facilitator asks the participant to navigate to a pre-built 'settings probe' HTML page (settings_probe.html, included in materials) containing one each of: an H1, an H2, a <nav> with aria-label, a <main>, and an <aside>. The wizard observes which elements are announced aloud and notes any that are suppressed. If landmark announcements are suppressed, the wizard disables all landmark_label_inject controls for that session and notes this in the session log. The co-design debrief is adjusted to exclude landmark-repair rule discussion for that participant.

## Materials needed

- 12 AI-generated news article HTML fixture files (4 per session × 3 sessions), each saved as a self-contained local HTML file (no external dependencies), generated using a large language model prompted to produce 600–800 word news articles on general-interest topics with intentionally variable heading hierarchy quality — specifically: 4 articles with hallucinated headings (heading text does not reflect section content), 4 articles with skipped heading levels (e.g., H2 to H5), and 4 articles with missing or ambiguous landmark labels — with one article per session containing a semantic confabulation detectable only by content reading (not tag inspection).
- Overlay JavaScript file (repair_overlay.js) implementing: (a) a MutationObserver that posts current landmark and heading focus state to a wizard panel via postMessage, (b) three DOM-rewriting functions triggered by wizard panel button clicks: applyHeadingRewrite(elementId, newTag, newText), injectLandmarkLabel(elementId, labelText), applyNestingFlatten(elementIdArray, newLevelArray), (c) a repairs_suppressed flag toggled by the repair_hold control, (d) a JSON event logger that appends to repair_log in localStorage and exports to a .json file on session end.
- Wizard control panel (wizard_panel.html): a single-page browser-based interface (running in a second Chrome window on the wizard's mirrored display) showing: (a) a live DOM outline of the current article updated every 500 ms, with pre-flagged elements highlighted in red, (b) four large buttons: HEADING REWRITE, LANDMARK LABEL INJECT, NESTING FLATTEN, REPAIR HOLD, each with a text input for free-text label entry when not using rule-based text, (c) a running repair event log panel, (d) a 60-second countdown timer that activates on repair_hold.
- repair_rules_[participantID]_session[N].json template — a structured JSON file with schema: {participant_id, session_number, rules: [{trigger_condition, repair_type, target_selector_hint, replacement_text, confidence_threshold}]} — pre-populated with an empty rules array for Session 1, and updated by the wizard within 48 hours after each session based on co-design debrief outcomes.
- Post-article Likert and open-ended rating form (rating_form.html): a keyboard-navigable HTML form with two 5-point Likert radio groups ('heading navigation orientation' and 'subsection search orientation'), two <textarea> fields for open-ended responses, a submit button that writes responses to a local JSON file named [participantID]_session[N]_article[M]_ratings.json, fully compatible with NVDA and VoiceOver (all inputs have explicit <label> elements, form uses <fieldset> and <legend> for grouping).
- Settings probe HTML page (settings_probe.html): a minimal HTML page containing exactly one H1 ('Settings Probe Page'), one H2 ('Navigation Landmarks'), one <nav> with aria-label='Primary Navigation', one <main> with aria-label='Main Content', and one <aside> with aria-label='Related Links' — used at session start to verify participant's screen-reader verbosity and landmark announcement settings.
- Structured observer observation sheet (printed, one per article per session): pre-printed A4 form with columns for timestamp, event type (from the 6-code vocabulary: EXPECT, CONFIRM, DISCONFIRM, RECOVER, ABANDON, REPAIR-NOTICE), free-text description, and a check box 'wizard repair active at this moment (Y/N).'
- Rule-card template (printed, minimum 10 copies per participant): A5 card with three labeled fields: 'Trigger condition (what DOM pattern or navigation behaviour should activate repair),' 'Repair action (heading rewrite / landmark label inject / nesting flatten / other),' 'Confidence threshold (always apply / apply only if participant navigates here / hold for participant confirmation).'
- Participant briefing script (printed, one copy per facilitator): a verbatim script covering study purpose (described as 'testing a web accessibility tool'), session structure, think-aloud instructions ('please say aloud what you are doing and what you expect to happen, even if it feels obvious'), and an explicit statement that the tool may make mistakes and the participant's job is to use it naturally and report their experience.
- Pre-session article review checklist (printed, one per article per session): a one-page form for the wizard to complete during the 24 hours before each session, documenting for each article: (a) all heading tags and their levels in DOM order, (b) all landmark elements and their ARIA labels or absence thereof, (c) wizard's pre-flagged elements for each of the three control types, (d) any elements identified as potential semantic confabulations requiring content reading to detect.
- Audio recording equipment: two cardioid microphones positioned to capture both participant speech and screen-reader speaker output simultaneously, connected to a portable audio recorder (minimum 16-bit, 44.1 kHz), with recordings labelled [participantID]_session[N]_audio.wav.
- Session 3 retrospective interview guide (printed): four structured interview questions as specified in task_flow step 5, with 2–3 follow-up probe questions per item (e.g., for question 2: 'Can you describe a specific moment from today's session where you expected the tool to help but it did not?'), plus space for facilitator to note participant-quoted descriptions of persistent failure types.
