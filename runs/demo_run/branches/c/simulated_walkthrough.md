## Session opening

A participant arriving for Session 1 would likely spend the first ten minutes in consent and onboarding, a window during which the facilitator's verbatim script would plausibly shape how the participant frames the rest of the study — in particular, the statement that "the tool may make mistakes" would reasonably prime the participant to attribute some breakdowns to the overlay rather than to their own navigation. Rehearsing this opening suggests the framing could cut both ways: a participant who internalises the "tool may be imperfect" framing might over-attribute confusion to the overlay in Sessions 2 and 3, while a participant who under-weights that caveat might internalise overlay failures as personal comprehension gaps. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The five-minute device orientation using `settings_probe.html` would likely be the first point at which the wizard and facilitator learn whether the participant's NVDA verbosity will render the landmark-repair arm of the study viable at all. Walking through this step mentally, a participant whose verbosity settings suppress landmark announcements would plausibly proceed through Session 1 without anyone noticing the mismatch, because Session 1 has no overlay active; the mismatch would only become consequential in Sessions 2 and 3 when `landmark_label_inject` fires into a silent channel. This suggests the device-check protocol carries more weight than its five-minute allocation implies. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant who has used NVDA daily for several years would reasonably expect the screen reader to behave as they have tuned it, and being asked to "not change settings" during the session could plausibly feel like a constraint on their ordinary expertise. Rehearsing this, one would anticipate that some participants might agree verbally but unconsciously adjust verbosity mid-session out of habit, which would be invisible to the wizard's mirrored feed unless the observer is watching for keystroke patterns. ## Task flow walkthrough

### Step 1 — heading navigation on the loaded article

Reaching step 1 in Session 1 (baseline), a participant using the H key to build a structural mental map would likely encounter the article's hallucinated headings and skipped levels in rapid succession, because heading-navigation is intentionally fast. Rehearsing this, the informative moment is plausibly not the heading itself but the micro-pause after it: a participant who continues pressing H without comment is probably tolerating the anomaly, while a participant who stops and re-navigates backwards is plausibly flagging a breakdown that may or may not surface verbally in think-aloud. The observer's 6-code scheme would have to catch this distinction within 30-second windows, which feels tight. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

In Sessions 2 and 3, the same step carries a timing problem the wizard has roughly 200 ms to solve. A participant pressing H at a typical experienced-user rate (conservatively 1–2 presses per second) would likely outrun the wizard's reaction latency on at least some headings, meaning the "batch mode vs. reactive" decision at page load is probably the single most consequential wizard decision in the entire study. Rehearsing this, one would expect early sessions with a given participant to favor reactive repair (because the co-designed ruleset is still sparse), which is precisely the condition under which timing failures are most likely to occur. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant asked to "locate and read aloud the third section heading" would reasonably interpret "third" against whatever structure the screen reader has announced — which, in overlay-active sessions, may differ from the source DOM. This creates an interpretive ambiguity the protocol does not obviously resolve: if the overlay has demoted an H2 to an H3 via `nesting_flatten_trigger`, is "the third section heading" counted pre- or post-repair? Rehearsing the instruction suggests the facilitator script may need a disambiguation clause. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 2 — subsection search with think-aloud

Step 2 is where the wizard's disorientation heuristic (three consecutive H or F6 presses without a "found it" signal) meets the participant's actual navigation style, and the fit is plausibly imperfect. A participant whose habitual strategy is a rapid heading scan followed by a mental pause would reasonably generate many short bursts of three-plus keystrokes that are not disorientation but routine orientation, and the wizard would have to discriminate between these in real time from a mirrored feed. Rehearsing this, one would expect the wizard to err toward over-intervention in early sessions and under-intervention later, which is itself a learning effect the protocol claims to measure — raising the concern that wizard learning and participant adaptation are confounded. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The system response in step 2 specifies that an injected ARIA label announces "at the next focus event or when the participant re-enters the region." A participant who has already moved past the region when the injection fires would likely never hear it on that pass, and whether they re-enter the region depends on their search strategy. Rehearsing this, one would plausibly predict that linear readers never hear most landmark injections while landmark-navigation users hear them inconsistently, concentrating the informative signal in a narrow behavioral band. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The three-minute "repair-resistant failure" threshold would likely feel long to a participant who has already decided they cannot find the target; a participant who verbally abandons at 90 seconds but is not offered a way out until the three-minute mark could plausibly experience the remaining 90 seconds as demoralising. Rehearsing the facilitator's position here, one would reasonably expect tension between the protocol's data-collection interest and the participant's experience of repeated failure. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 3 — Likert rating and open-ended reflection

Reaching the rating form, a participant would likely carry over the emotional tone of the preceding navigation task into their Likert rating, such that the rating may index the final moment of the task more than the task as a whole. Rehearsing the 5-point scale, one would plausibly predict that participants who recovered successfully at the end of a difficult article rate higher than participants whose article was uniformly easy but ended with a minor stumble — a within-session article-ordering artifact that the analysis plan's "within-session variance" metric would not obviously distinguish from overlay effects. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The open-ended prompt "describe the moment, if any, when the page structure felt wrong or unexpected" would plausibly elicit different responses depending on whether the participant experienced an overlay repair as a repair or as a spontaneous page behavior. A participant who does not connect the mid-navigation DOM change to the overlay might describe the change itself as "wrong or unexpected," which would enter the analysis as evidence of disorientation rather than as evidence of successful (but jarring) repair. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 4 — co-design debrief

In the debrief, a participant is asked to reconstruct "what did you hear, what did you expect to hear" from a timestamped excerpt the observer has selected. Rehearsing this, one would reasonably expect the selection itself to shape the co-design output: excerpts chosen by the observer are the moments the observer noticed, which skews toward breakdowns that were audible or verbalised and away from silent breakdowns (for example, landmark injections the participant never heard). The rule changes that emerge are plausibly a partial reflection of the participant's navigation experience, weighted toward the moments most legible to an outside observer. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

A participant proposing a repair rule in natural language and then hearing the wizard's implementability assessment at the start of the next session would reasonably develop an implicit model of "what the tool can do," which would plausibly constrain the kinds of rules they propose in later sessions. Rehearsing the three-session arc, one would expect rule proposals to become more technically tractable and less exploratory across sessions — which could be interpreted as convergence or as the participant learning to self-censor, and the protocol does not obviously distinguish these. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

### Step 5 — Session 3 retrospective

The retrospective questions (especially question 2, "which types of problem persisted") depend on the participant's ability to remember across three sessions separated by at least five days each. Rehearsing this, a participant who is offered the aggregate repair-log summary as a memory aid would plausibly anchor their answers to the categories the log makes visible, while a participant who does not receive the summary would plausibly answer from the most recent session's experience. The wizard's default to share the summary is reasonable but would shape the retrospective into a somewhat log-structured reflection rather than a free recall. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

Question 3 ("moments when the overlay made navigation harder") would likely be the hardest for a participant to answer honestly if they have developed rapport with the research team over three sessions. Rehearsing the social dynamics, one would plausibly expect polite under-reporting, and the facilitator's probe questions may need explicit permission-granting framing ("we especially want to hear about moments when it made things worse") to counteract this. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Friction hotspots

- Wizard reaction latency against habitual screen-reader navigation speed — a participant pressing H or F6 at experienced-user rates would plausibly encounter several headings or landmarks faster than the wizard can anticipate and trigger a repair, concentrating timing failures in the very conditions the study most wants to examine. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- The distinction between "disorientation" and "routine fast scanning" in the wizard's heuristic — three consecutive keystrokes without a verbal "found it" is plausibly indistinguishable from ordinary skimming for some participants, which would produce wizard over-intervention in early sessions. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Semantic confabulations that are invisible to the DOM-layer repair architecture — a participant who encounters a heading whose level is correct but whose text contradicts its body would plausibly experience disorientation the overlay cannot touch, and the coding scheme would not register the event as a heading-disorientation. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Silent landmark injections under non-default NVDA verbosity — if the settings probe catches this, the landmark arm is reduced for that participant; if it misses, the wizard would plausibly mis-read null response as successful repair. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Confounding of wizard learning with participant adaptation across sessions — both parties improve over three sessions, and the protocol's analysis would have trouble separating "the overlay worked better in Session 3" from "the wizard got better at operating it" or "the participant got better at tolerating it." [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
- Social desirability in the co-design debrief and retrospective — a participant who has spent three sessions with the research team would plausibly under-report moments of overlay-induced harm, especially on question 3 of the retrospective. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

## Risks to interpretation

Because articles differ across sessions, the protocol correctly avoids absolute time comparisons and uses within-session SD as a proxy for overlay consistency. Rehearsing this choice, one would note that SD is sensitive to article-structural variability that is nominally controlled (hallucinated headings, skipped levels, missing labels are distributed across articles) but is in practice hard to equalise — an article with a single severe anomaly may dominate the SD regardless of overlay activity. Interpretation of SD trajectories would plausibly need to be cross-referenced with the per-article repair-event density before any consistency claim is advanced. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The "apparent resolution rate" metric (proportion of repairs followed within 10 seconds by a CONFIRM code or absence of DISCONFIRM) treats the absence of disconfirmation as evidence of resolution. Rehearsing this, one would note that silence after a repair could also mean the participant did not hear the repair (the NVDA verbosity case), did not connect the repair to their task, or was still processing. The metric would plausibly conflate genuine resolution with silent non-reception, particularly for `landmark_label_inject`. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

With six participants and three sessions each, the study generates a rich longitudinal per-participant record but a small cross-participant sample, which the protocol appropriately frames as co-design rather than inferential. Rehearsing what conclusions would be defensible, one would reasonably expect the strongest output to be a typology of repair-resistant failure categories and a set of participant-proposed rules with implementability annotations — claims about prevalence or efficacy across the BLV screen-reader user population would outrun what six cases can support. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]

The rule-card template's three fields (trigger / repair action / confidence threshold) would plausibly constrain the space of proposable rules to ones that fit the template's ontology, which mirrors the overlay's existing capabilities. Rehearsing this, one would expect the template to under-elicit rule proposals that fall outside DOM-layer repair — and since those proposals are precisely the evidence for the "limits of post-hoc structural correction" sub-claim, the instrument may under-sample the very phenomenon it aims to characterise. [SIMULATION_REHEARSAL]
